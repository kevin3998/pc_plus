
## Abstract

This study developed mathematical prediction models to predict the RMS roughness value (Rq) and water contact angle (WCA) values of cadmium-based superhydrophobic transparent conductive oxide surfaces. An experimental design approach was used to optimise the process parameters in the SILAR deposition of CdO films. Validation experiments were carried out under selected random conditions to validate the developed mathematical models. The obtained mathematical models not only predicted the roughness and contact angle values with high accuracy but were also used to determine the optimum process parameters to obtain the most effective surface roughness and contact angle. The results indicated that the developed mathematical models can predict the Rq and WCA values of the synthesized transparent conductive oxide surfaces both under optimum conditions and under randomly selected conditions with relative error varying between 0.6 and 4.7%. The Rq and WCA of the most effective cadmium-based superhydrophobic transparent conductive oxide surface synthesised under optimum conditions were measured as to be 189.1 nm and 151.15°, respectively, and were estimated by the developed models to be 193.3 nm and 150.28°, respectively. The closeness of the experimental and predicted values obtained for both models demonstrated the reliability of the developed mathematical models.

## 1 Introduction

Nowadays, the environmental, industrial, and technological applications of nanostructured composites and oxide particles are increasing [ 1 , 2 , 3 , 4 , 5 ]. The wetting behavior of a nanostructured solid surface is of wide interest due to its many practical applications such as self-cleaning, water/oil separation, anti-icing, anti-corrosion, anti-fouling and self-healing [ 6 , 7 , 8 , 9 , 10 ]. Whether a surface is superhydrophilic, hydrophilic, hydrophobic, or superhydrophobic depends on the contact angle of water with the surface. These definitions are used to characterise the wettability of surfaces. Superhydrophilic surfaces are those with a contact angle of less than 5°. On these types of surfaces, water spreads over the surface and completely wets the surface. The contact angle of hydrophilic surfaces is less than 90°. On these surfaces, instead of spreading completely, water stays on the surface at a very small angle and partially wets the surface. Surfaces with a water contact angle between 90° and 150° are called hydrophobic, and surfaces with a contact angle greater than 150° are called superhydrophobic surfaces [ 11 , 12 ]. These surfaces also have a very low (CAH < 10) water contact angle hysteresis (CAH). Thus, the water droplet easily rolls off from the superhydrophobic surface when tilted at a small angle [ 13 ]. While hydrophobic surfaces show less wetting than hydrophilic surfaces, the water droplet on superhydrophobic surfaces takes on an almost spherical structure. Superhydrophobic surfaces can be created on various materials such as glass, metal, wood, and cotton, depending on the application.
To date, some research has been done on the wetting behavior of transparent conductive oxide nanomaterials [ 14 ]. Among various semiconductor materials, cadmium-based (CdO), transparent conductive oxide surfaces stand out due to their structural, optical, and electrical properties [ 15 ]. The superhydrophobic properties of cadmium-based transparent conductive oxide surfaces are extremely important due to applications such as corrosion inhibition and anti-reflective surfaces [ 16 , 17 , 18 ]. Different methods have been proposed in the literature for the synthesis of nanostructured particles and cadmium oxide thin films [ 19 , 20 , 21 , 22 , 23 , 24 , 25 ]. Besides methods such as sol–gel, CBD, spray pyrolysis, and CVD, SILAR is a simple, low-cost and easy method that can be used to fabricate superhydrophobic CdO surfaces. In this method, various parameters such as temperature, pH, precursor concentrations, and deposition time are effective on the morphological properties of the surface [ 26 , 27 ]. The performance of superhydrophobic films in nanoscale applications is strongly dependent on the surface roughness of the film in many areas [ 8 , 28 ]. The empirical equation to be established between the cadmium-based superhydrophobic transparent conductive oxide coating parameters and the roughness and water contact angle values of the synthesized surface can provide a significant advantage in nanoscale applications. Although researchers have previously examined both ZnO and CuO superhydrophobic surfaces, CdO superhydrophobic thin film surfaces have not been investigated [ 29 , 30 , 31 , 32 , 33 , 34 , 35 ]. The models we propose in this study can calculate the surface roughness and water contact angle of CdO superhydrophobic thin films before the SILAR process. Calculating roughness and wettability properties based on deposition parameters before the synthesis of CdO thin films can offer many innovations in terms of applications such as self-cleaning, anti-reflective surfaces, and corrosion. Besides as far as we know, there is no study in the literature on using design of experiments (DOE)-based multiple regression analysis to optimize the parameters pH, annealing temperature, and immersion time in stearic acid in the SILAR deposition process of CdO superhydrophobic films. The experimental design approach is an effective technique in terms of both evaluating the interaction between synthesis parameters and saving time and cost [ 29 , 36 , 37 , 38 , 39 , 40 , 41 ].
In our study, we focused on creating mathematical models that can calculate the surface roughness and water contact angle of CdO superhydrophobic thin films before the SILAR process and determining the optimum levels of process parameters in the SILAR deposition process of CdO with a chemometric approach. Therefore, in this study, response surface methodology (RSM), an important type of DOE, was used to determine the optimum levels of process parameters that have a strong impact on the roughness and wettability performance of superhydrophobic CdO surfaces. In addition, mathematical prediction models based on deposition parameters were developed to predict the surface roughness (Rq) and water contact angle (WCA) of cadmium-based superhydrophobic transparent conductive oxide films using mathematical modelling technique.

## 2 Experimental section


### 2.1 Materials

While cadmium acetate dihydrate (reagent grade, 98%) was used as the cadmium source for coating CdO thin films on the slide, ammonia solution (25% for analysis) was used as the complexing agent. Stearic acid (reagent grade, 95%) was used for surface modification. Glass bases with dimensions of 76 mm × 26 mm × 1 mm were used as base material. The solutions prepared and used during laboratory studies were prepared with deionized water. Dilute sulphuric acid (H 2 SO 4 :H 2 O, 1:5 by volume), deionized water and acetone were used for 10 min to clean the slides used as substrates. All chemicals were acquired from Sigma Aldrich Co. Chemicals and solvents did not need further purification and were used directly.

### 2.2 Preparation of CdO thin films

In the SILAR process, 100 mL of 0.1 M cadmium acetate dihydrate (Cd(CH 3 COO) 2 ⋅2H 2 O) was used as the cationic solution. Ammonia solution was added dropwise to the constantly stirring cationic solution until pH values in the range of 10.2 to 11.8 (according to Table 1 ) were obtained. HACH sensation 1 portable pH meter was used to determine the pH levels of cationic solutions. The glass slide was then immersed in cationic solution at 85 °C for 30 s and then immersed in deionized water at 85 °C for 30 s. Thin films prepared at the end of 30 SILAR cycles were annealed in air for 1 h at the annealing temperature levels created for the response surface design (Table 2 ). A schematic diagram of the cadmium-based superhydrophobic transparent conductive oxide fabrication process is shown in Fig. 1 .

### 2.3 Surface modification

Stearic acid was used to obtain superhydrophobic cadmium oxide thin film surfaces. For surface modification, an 8 mM ethanolic solution of stearic acid was prepared. CdO thin films coated on glass slides with the SILAR method were kept in the stearic acid solution for the immersion times shown in Table 2 (range 0.6 to 7.4 h). A handheld digital stopwatch with an accuracy of 0.01 s was used to measure immersion time. When determining the immersion time in stearic acid, measurements were taken in minutes. The samples with completed surface modification were dried at room temperature and stored in the dark for characterization.

### 2.4 Characterization

To evaluate the surface properties of the synthesised cadmium oxide thin films, SEM images were taken using a scanning electron microscope device (Thermo Fisher Scientific Apreo S). The instrument was operated at room temperature in the range of 0.2–30 kV. An X-ray diffractometer (Rigaku SmartLab) operating in the range of 20–60 kV and 2–50 mA was used to detect X-ray diffraction patterns that provide important information about the structural properties of the samples. In order to observe the bond structures of the synthesized CdO thin films and the surface modified CdO thin films and the groups in the material, FTIR analyzes of the samples were performed with a fourier transform infrared spectroscopy device (PerkinElmer Spectrum Two). Scanning Probe Microscopy (SPM) technique and Gwydion 2.50 software were used to determine the surface topography of the samples. Topography information of the samples was obtained with nanometer precision. The water contact angles of the surface modified CdO films were determined via ImageJ software using the LB-ADSA (low bond axisymmetric drop shape analysis) method. WCA measurements were made at five different points on each sample and the results are presented with the mean and standard deviation.

## 3 Results and discussion


### 3.1 Mathematical prediction model building

A three-factor, five-level central composite design with the response surface method was used in optimizing the production parameters of cadmium oxide semiconductor thin films and in the modelling study. In the central composite design consisting of 20 experiments, pH, annealing temperature, and immersion time in stearic acid were selected as independent variables. The DOE table used in the chemometric optimisation and modelling study and the experimental and predicted values of the roughness and wettability responses are given in Table 2 . According to the experimental results, the RMS roughness values for CdO thin films varied between 168.1 and 215.9 nm. SEM images corresponding to CdO thin film samples synthesised according to central composite design experiments (Run 1–20) are shown in Fig. 2 . From the raw and stearic acid-modified surface images, it was observed that the cadmium oxide semiconductor thin films were completely coated on the glass substrates, and a highly homogeneous surface morphology was formed. SPM images of CdO films grown under the experimental conditions specified in the CCD table are given in Fig. 3 . It has been observed that the topographic images of the semiconductor thin films are compatible with the RMS roughness values of the synthesised materials (Table 2 ). The water contact angle values measured after surface modification of CdO semiconductor thin films were between 145.12 degrees and 155.73 degrees. Water contact angle images of CdO semiconductor films obtained from 20 sets of CdO SILAR coating experiments are given in Fig. 4 . In the WCA images, water took the form of drops on all CdO surfaces modified with stearic acid. It was observed that runs 1, 2, 3, 4, 6, 10, 11, and 13, which exhibited water contact angles over 150°, created superhydrophobic surfaces, while other runs (5, 7, 8, 9, 12, 14, 15, 16, 17, 18, 19, and 20) were observed to have hydrophobic character. According to the rolling angle test results for surfaces 1, 2, 3, 4, 6, 10, 11, and 13, it was observed that the roll-off angle of the surfaces was below 3°. The second-order polynomial equations developed to predict the RMS roughness and water contact angle values of cadmium oxide semiconductor thin films and the regression coefficients calculated for each parameter are given in Table 3 . In the mathematical prediction models created to predict the Rq and WCA values of cadmium oxide semiconductor thin films, pH, annealing temperature and immersion time in stearic acid are expressed with the symbols A, B, and C, respectively. The relationship between the measured experimental values (squares) and the values predicted from the model (line) for RMS roughness and water contact angle are shown in Figs. 5 a, 6 a, respectively. In Table 2 and Figs. 5 a, 6 a, the predicted data for the Rq and WCA values of CdO thin films are compatible with the experimentally measured values. Another indicator of the compatibility of the data are that the R 2 values of the mathematical models created to predict the Rq and WCA values were 0.9855 and 0.9543, respectively.
The suitability of the second-order model equations developed to predict the RMS roughness and water contact angle values of cadmium oxide semiconductor thin films synthesised by the SILAR method was determined using ANOVA analysis. The ANOVA results for the roughness and wettability responses are given in Tables 4 , 5 , respectively. From the F-values and probability values in the ANOVA tables, it is understood that both mathematical prediction models created to predict the Rq and WCA values of cadmium oxide semiconductor thin films are valid. In addition, the R 2 values obtained for the developed prediction models are close to 1, which is another indicator of the accuracy of the models [ 42 ]. The probability values of the LOF (lack of fit) data shown in Tables 4 , 5 for the Rq and WCA models are greater than 0.05. This showed that the lack of fit was not significant for both statistical prediction models. In the ANOVA table obtained for the Rq prediction model (Table 4 ), it was seen that the model terms A, C, AB, BC, A 2 , B 2 , C 2 with probability values less than 0.05 were significant. When the ANOVA table of the WCA prediction model given in Table 5 is examined, it is understood that the probability values of the terms B, C, AB, AC, BC, A 2 , B 2 , C 2 are less than 0.05, that is, the terms have a significant effect on WCA. ANOVA analysis of the RMS roughness prediction model showed that pH and immersion time in stearic acid had a significant effect on the Rq value. The ANOVA result obtained for WCA revealed that WCA was significantly affected by the annealing temperature and the immersion time in stearic acid in the model developed to predict the water contact angle. Surface roughness can change the wettability character of materials [ 43 ]. In the synthesis of CdO thin films with SILAR, the effect of pH and annealing temperature on material roughness cannot be ignored. From a viewpoint of the hydrophobicity not only pH and annealing temperature but also the immersion time in stearic acid were found to be effective parameters. Because both surface structure and surface energy are related to film wettability [ 30 ].
According to the statistical results of the developed mathematical prediction models in Table 6 , the coefficient of determination ( R 2 ), predicted coefficient of determination (pred- R 2 ) and adjusted coefficient of determination (adj- R 2 ) data of Rq and WCA were found to be greater than 80%. This showed that the models were compatible and that the response variables were adequately explained by the models [ 44 ]. On the other hand, when the PRESS values of the models were compared, it was seen that the prediction ability of the model developed to predict WCA was better than the Rq model [ 45 ]. The coefficient of variance (C.V.%) calculated from the standard deviation and mean value was determined as 1.05 and 0.61 for the Rq and WCA models, respectively. According to the results obtained, C.V.% value below 10% implies better reproducibility as well as higher precision and reliability [ 46 ]. Adequate precision for both the Rq (33.9299) and WCA (16.0279) models was found to be above 4, indicating adequate model discrimination of both prediction equations [ 47 ].
The normality of the data were evaluated from the normal probability graphs of the residuals drawn using Rq and WCA data. The normal probability plots of the residuals drawn for Rq and WCA values are shown in Figs. 5 b, 6 b, respectively. The fact that the residuals are distributed close to a straight line for both data demonstrates that the errors are normally distributed [ 48 ]. In the plots of residuals versus predicted data for RMS roughness and water contact angle values of cadmium oxide semiconductor thin films (Figs. 5 c, 6 c), all points are randomly distributed within the limits (horizontal line value of ± 3.00). This showed that the models created for both experimental data were sufficient and that no violations in data distribution occurred [ 41 , 49 ]. The graphs of the residuals drawn for Rq and WCA values according to the experimental run order are given in Figs. 5 d, 6 d, respectively. When the graphs were examined, which allowed controlling the hidden variables that may have affected the response, the observed random distributions proved the suitability of the Rq and WCA models [ 50 ].

### 3.2 Effect of the independent variables on RMS roughness (Rq) and water contact angle (WCA)

The extent to which the Rq and WCA values of the cadmium oxide semiconductor thin films were affected by independent variables was evaluated using response surface graphs. Figure 7 shows the effects of pH, annealing temperature, and stearic acid holding time on the RMS Roughness. The response surface graphs show that pH and immersion time in stearic acid had a significant effect on the change in the Rq value. In Fig. 7 a, it is observed that the increase in pH value causes a decrease in roughness in regions where the annealing temperature is low. In regions where the annealing temperature is high, the increase in pH value did not create a significant difference in the Rq value until pH 11, however, when this value was exceeded, the increase in pH value increased the Rq value. Especially in the high pH region, increasing the annealing temperature resulted in an increase in the RMS Roughness. It is well-known that pH and annealing temperature can change surface structure and morphology. Articles reporting the effect of solution pH and annealing temperature on the surface roughness properties of CdO are available in the literature. Azizar Rahman and Khan [ 51 ] observed that the surface roughness of the CdO films increases with the increase of annealing temperature. Increasing the annealing temperature provides the energy required for the coalescence of CdO nuclei, resulting in a larger grain size and therefore a rougher surface. On the other hand, İsmail et al. [ 52 ] reported that increasing the pH led to a decrease in the number of clusters formed by the coalescence of small CdO grains and a decrease in grain size, which resulted in a decrease in surface roughness. Additionally, when the effects of pH and stearic acid immersion time parameters given in Fig. 7 b on the Rq value are examined, it can be seen that the increase in pH value causes a decrease in RMS roughness at both low and high stearic acid immersion times. This result is consistent with the effect of pH on the Rq value observed by İsmail et al. [ 52 ]. On the other hand, it has been observed that the increase in STA immersion time in the low pH region tended to increase the RMS roughness. The increase in surface roughness can be attributed to the deposition of more stearate crystals on the surface. Gurav et al. [ 31 ] reported similar behavior on ZnO surface modified with varying amounts of stearic acid. In the high pH region, it can be seen that the increase in STA immersion time causes a slight decrease in Rq up to 4 h, and when the 4 h value is exceeded, the trend is reversed. This can be explained by the fact that initially the high pH value is dominant and the effect of stearic acid is limited, and after 4 h are exceeded, stearic acid shows the expected effect.
Figure 7 c shows the effect of annealing temperature and stearic acid immersion time on the RMS roughness. As shown in Fig. 7 c, the increase in annealing temperature resulted in an increase in the surface roughness of the thin film. This result is similar to the pattern obtained in Fig. 7 a. As explained above, the main reasons for the increase in roughness are better crystallization of the thin films and obtaining larger grain size by providing additional energy to promote the coalescence of adjacent grains. The similar behaviour was reported by Krishna et al. [ 53 ]. On the other hand, like the result obtained in Fig. 7 b, the increase in STA immersion time caused an increase in RMS roughness. Increasing the STA immersion time may increase the possibility of further deposition of stearate crystals on the surface, which affects the surface roughness [ 34 , 54 ].
Three-dimensional diagrams showing the effects of pH-annealing temperature and STA immersion time parameters on the water contact angle value are presented in Fig. 8 . In Fig. 8 a, it is observed that the increase in pH causes an increase in the water contact angle in regions where the annealing temperature is low. Contrary to expectation, the increase in contact angle observed with increasing pH suggests that the stearic acid factor is dominant, and the pH-dependent roughness effect remains at a limited level. On the other hand, in regions where the annealing temperature was high, the increase in pH value decreased the WCA value. The Rq decreases with increasing pH has been reported by İsmail et al. [ 52 ]. The decrease in water contact angle can be attributed to the decrease in roughness. If the change in water contact angle value is evaluated in terms of the increase in annealing temperature, it is concluded that the WCA value increases with the increase of T at low pH, and that at high pH, the WCA tends to decrease depending on the annealing temperature. Ziabari and Ghodsi [ 55 ] reported similar observation regarding the increase in film roughness with increasing annealing temperature. The increase in water contact angle is the result of the increase in surface roughness with the increase in annealing temperature. The reason for the decrease in WCA value in the high pH region may be that the pH factor is more dominant than the annealing temperature.
The response surface graphs drawn with the WCA, pH, and STA immersion time parameters are given in Fig. 8 b. It was observed that the increase in pH value increased the WCA value, especially in the region where the STA immersion time was high. The reason for this may be the change in Rq value as the level of stearic acid on the surface increases [ 31 ]. However, increasing the STA immersion time at both low and high pH caused a slight decrease in WCA. As a result of increasing the retention time in stearic acid, excess stearic acid may have reduced the surface roughness by filling the rough micro-nano surface. Similar behavior was reported by Murtaza et al. [ 56 ].
Figure 8 c shows the effect of annealing temperature and STA immersion time on water contact angle. It can be observed from the three-dimensional diagram that increasing the annealing temperature caused the water contact angle to increase similar to the result obtained in Fig. 8 b. A slight decrease in WCA was observed with the increase of T in the region where the STA immersion time was high. This may be because excess stearic acid fills the nanoscale surface, reducing surface roughness [ 56 ]. Additionally, it was observed that the increase in STA immersion time at low annealing temperatures caused a slight increase in WCA. This can be attributed to the fact that stearic acid increases the surface roughness. In the region where the annealing temperature is high, the increase in STA immersion time decreased the water contact angle slightly. This decrease in water contact angle may be due to the excess of stearic acid reducing the surface roughness, similar to the result in Fig. 8 b.

### 3.3 Optimum conditions

Optimum synthesis parameters of cadmium-based superhydrophobic transparent conductive oxide films were developed from the surface roughness and water contact angle prediction models (Table 3 ). The optimum values determined by the mathematical model designed to predict the RMS roughness are 10.5, 388.5 °C and 2.1 h for pH, annealing temperature and immersion time in stearic acid, respectively. The RMS roughness value of CdO produced under optimum synthesis conditions was measured to be 189.1 nm. If the determined optimum model term values (A:10.5, B:388.5 °C and C:2.1 h) are written into the Rq prediction model (Table 3 ), it can be seen that the mathematical model predicts the RMS roughness value to be 193.3 nm. The optimum values calculated by the mathematical model developed to predict the water contact angle value are: 10.6 for pH, 408.9 °C for annealing temperature, and 2.6 h for immersion time in stearic acid. The experimental WCA value of the CdO film synthesised under optimized process conditions (A:10.6, B:408.9 °C and C:2.6 h) was measured to be 151.15° (Fig. 11 c), and it has been predicted to be 150.28° according to the WCA mathematical model (Table 3 ). The obtained data show that mathematical prediction models can predict RMS roughness and water contact angle values with a relative error of 2.2% and 0.6%, respectively (Table 8 ). The closeness of the experimental and predicted values obtained for both mathematical models proved the reliability of the developed mathematical prediction models.

### 3.4 Structural properties

XRD analysis was used to examine the crystallographic structure of the thin film we produced and to prove the formation of the CdO phase. The XRD graph of the CdO semiconductor thin film obtained by synthesis under optimum pH, annealing temperature and immersion time in stearic acid is shown in Fig. 9 in the diffraction angles (2 θ ) between 20° and 80° in steps of 0.02°. When the XRD graph of the optimised CdO thin film was evaluated, we saw that the XRD pattern of the film was formed in a polycrystalline cubic CdO structure without any impurities. Also, the CdO diffraction peaks are well fitted with the JCPDS diffraction data (JCPDS:01-080-4437). The diffraction pattern of the optimized CdO thin film showed obvious diffraction peaks at 32.93°, 38.24°, 55.22°, 65.84° and 69.22°, which were attributed to the preferred orientations (111), (200), (220), (311) and (222). It was observed that the (111) and (200) diffraction peaks were more dominant than the other diffraction peaks [(220), (311) and (222)]. The average crystallite size of the optimised CdO thin film was calculated as 10.8 nm from the (111), (200), (220), (311) and (222) X-ray diffraction peaks using the Debye–Scherrer formula [ 57 , 58 ]. As it is known, the average grain size of the obtained samples is quite effective on the electronic properties [ 59 ]. The change in grain size due to the change in the production parameters may result in a shift in the band gap value. In general, the decrease in the band gap value is attributed to the increase in grain size [ 39 ]. On the other hand, a larger grain size will result in a rougher surface. As a result, it is known that the increase in roughness on hydrophobic surfaces increases the contact angle and decreases wettability [ 43 ]. In addition, from the EDS measurement of the CdO sample prepared under optimum conditions, the atomic percentage values for Cadmium (Cd) and Oxygen (O) were found to be 43.90% and 56.10%, respectively, and it was determined that compositions with oxygen content close to the stoichiometric value were produced [ 60 ].

### 3.5 FTIR analysis

FTIR spectra of CdO and stearic acid-modified CdO thin films are given in Fig. 10 . The peaks at 1550, 2850, and 2919 cm −1 that appear in the stearic acid-modified CdO thin film spectrum instead of the CdO spectrum indicate that stearic acid is grafted onto the CdO surface [ 52 ]. The low-intensity peak observed at 1550 cm −1 can be attributed to the COO-stretching vibration of the cadmium oxide semiconductor thin film. This indicated the presence of stearic acid in the CdO nanoparticles [ 32 , 33 ]. In addition, the peaks observed at wavelengths of 2850 cm −1 and 2919 cm −1 in the FTIR spectrum of the CdO semiconductor thin film synthesized under optimized experimental conditions were attributed to –CH 3 and –CH 2 asymmetric and symmetric stretching vibrations, respectively [ 30 , 34 , 56 ].

### 3.6 Topographical and morphological properties

SEM images of the superhydrophobic CdO semiconductor thin film obtained under optimum pH, annealing temperature and immersion time in stearic acid are presented in Fig. 11 a, b. In the obtained SEM images, it can be clearly seen that the morphology of the CdO thin films before modification is significantly different from the films after modification with stearic acid. While the SEM image of the raw CdO thin film showed flake, plate and nanorod-like morphologies, the modified CdO thin film surface clearly showed the presence of stearic acid coated on these structures. It is known that the surface morphologies of semiconductor oxide thin films are affected by experimental synthesis parameters [ 26 ]. It has been observed that the cadmium oxide thin film surface shapes obtained in accordance with the CCD design form nanoflake-like structures, especially at low pH values, and generally exhibit vertically oriented nanostructures (flake and plate-like) at high pH values (Fig. 2 ). On the other hand, unlike CdO films annealed at low temperatures, the porous flake-like structures observed in the film morphology annealed at high temperatures are thought to result from the difference in annealing temperature.
SPM image of CdO film grown under the optimized experimental conditions (Run 21) are given in Fig. 11 d. From the three-dimensional surface topography obtained for the superhydrophobic CdO semiconductor thin film, the film surface is quite rough. The RMS roughness value measured for the CdO film synthesized under optimum parameter conditions was found to be compatible with the film surface obtained from the SPM image (Table 8 ). However, the wettability properties of nanostructures coated on the substrate can be related to both the chemical nature of the nanostructures and their roughness properties. Surface wettability, which involves the interaction between a liquid and a solid in contact, provides information about the interaction of the material with water. The water contact angle of the CdO sample synthesized under optimum conditions measured as 151.15° (Fig. 11 c) showed that a surface with superhydrophobic properties was obtained.

### 3.7 Verification of the developed mathematical models

Validation experiments of the second-order polynomial equations developed to predict the RMS roughness and water contact angle values of cadmium oxide semiconductor thin films were carried out both with the optimum parameter combination (Run 21) and application experiments (Run 22, Run 23, Run 24) in which the parameter combination was randomly selected. SEM images (before and after stearic acid modification), water contact angle images and SPM images of CdO semiconductor thin films grown in randomly selected parameter combinations are given in Fig. 12 . The experimental and predicted results of CdO thin films synthesised under the conditions of optimum parameter combination and randomly selected parameter combinations are shown in Table 8 . As shown in Fig. 13 , the water droplets on the CdO thin film surfaces synthesised in the optimised CdO sample (Run 21) and in the application experiments (Run 22, Run 23, Run 24) exhibited water contact angles between 148.38° and 154.79°. The application experiments have shown that both the RMS roughness and the water contact angle prediction model can predict the Rq and WCA values of CdO films with a relative error below 5%. The fact that a good agreement was obtained between the predicted and measured values emphasizes the reliability of the developed mathematical prediction models.

## 4 Conclusions

In the present study, the SILAR method was used to prepare cadmium-based superhydrophobic transparent conductive oxide surfaces. The effect of process parameters on the roughness and water contact angle of CdO surfaces was evaluated with the help of a 3-factor, 5-level central composite design. Mathematical prediction models based on deposition parameters were developed to predict the surface roughness and water contact angle of cadmium oxide semiconductor thin films. The RMS roughness value of the CdO thin film surface prepared under optimum synthesis conditions was measured to be 189.1 nm and showed superhydrophobic character with a WCA of 151.15°. The predicted RMS roughness and water contact angle values for the optimised CdO sample were estimated to be 193.3 nm and 150.28°, respectively, using the developed mathematical models. The closeness of the experimental and predicted values in the application experiments carried out with randomly selected parameter combinations confirmed the reliability of the developed mathematical models. The Rq and WCA values of CdO semiconductor thin films, both synthesised under optimum synthesis conditions and grown with randomly selected parameter combinations, could be predicted by the developed mathematical models with a relative error ranging from 0.6 to 4.7%. The effects of independent variables on the water contact angle and roughness of superhydrophobic cadmium oxide thin films were evaluated with response surface graphs. In general, an increase in annealing temperature increased the roughness similar to the STA effect, while an increase in pH decreased the roughness by affecting the grain size. In the context of the hydrophobic character of the solid surface, the increase in roughness resulted in an increase in the contact angle and thus a decrease in wettability. The findings provided a better understanding of the effects of pH, annealing temperature, and immersion time in stearic acid on the surface roughness and wettability of CdO films. The ability to predict the surface roughness and water contact angle of cadmium-based superhydrophobic transparent conductive oxide films based on deposition parameters prior to the SILAR process could provide a significant advantage in nanoscale applications such as self-cleaning, anti-reflective surfaces, and corrosion inhibition.
