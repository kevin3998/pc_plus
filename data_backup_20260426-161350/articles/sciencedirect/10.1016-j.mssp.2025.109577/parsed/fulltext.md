
## 1. Introduction

Transparent Conductive Oxide (TCO) is a vital component of electrically conductive materials with minimal light absorption. Utilized in thin film technologies, TCO finds application in various optoelectronic devices, including displays, solar cells, and light-emitting diodes (LEDs). As a wide-bandgap oxide semiconductor , typically surpassing 3.2 eV, TCO exhibits a high concentration of free electrons in the conduction band. Material defects or extrinsic dopants positioned near the conduction band edge contribute to these characteristics. Due to the elevated electron carrier concentration, electromagnetic radiation is absorbed within the visible and infrared spectrum, with particular importance placed on the visible range. Achieving optimal performance necessitates a delicate balance between electrical conductivity and optical transmittance, typically targeted at a transmittance level of 90 % [ 1 ].
The rising demand for transparent and flexible electronic devices, essential for the next generation's lightweight, flexible, and portable gadgets, highlights the critical role of TCOs such as ITO . Historically, commercially available electronics have heavily relied on ITO-coated polymer substrates, valued for their unique blend of optical transparency, electrical conductivity, and manufacturing simplicity. In addition to ITO , various TCO types, including doped metal oxides like ZnO:Al, thin metal layers, dielectric/metal/dielectric multilayers, metal grids, transparent conductive polymers, and nanoscale materials such as graphene, carbon nanotubes (CNTs), and metal nanowires (NWs), are utilized in optoelectronics. High optoelectronic device performance requires high optical transparency in designated spectral regions and low sheet resistance.
This paper explores the progress of flexible TCO film development with perovskite structure using a systematic review approach. The selection of flexible substrate TCO characteristics are discussed. Different perovskite structures and deposition methods are further discussed based on their excellent physical and electrical properties. The classification of perovskite and its current structure used as TCO, besides being attached to the flexible substrate are discussed in this paper. The advantages and disadvantages of the deposition method in developed TCO thin films are discussed to improve the TCO performance. The industrial considerations of flexible perovskite TCO by considering the present challenges and opportunities were highlighted in this paper review. Based on researchers’ work, the industrial need for flexible device applications to optoelectronic device technology are elaborated.
This paper aims to explore the progress development of flexible TCO film with perovskite structure by using a systematic review approach. The selection of flexible substrate, different types of perovskite structures and deposition methods should be further discussed based on their excellent physical and electrical properties.

### 1.1. History

The TCO revolution traces back to the early 1900s when Badeker [ 2 ] introduced cadmium oxide (CdO) in solar cells, prepared via thermal oxidation of a vacuum-sputtered film. However, its limited use stemmed from toxicity concerns and less effective electron mass despite its high electron mobility . Subsequently, in the 1940s, tin oxide ( Sn O 2 ) TCO films marked the initial phase of film production, utilizing techniques like spray pyrolysis and chemical vapor deposition (CVD) for electroluminescent panel applications. The development progressed further in the next 50 years, with tin-doped indium oxide (In 2 O 3 :Sn1), commonly known as ITO, emerging as a transparent electrode material extensively utilized in flat panel displays (FPDs). In the late 1950s and early 1960s, ITO was mostly deposited on In/Sn metal using DC diodes for vacuum evaporation and RF sputtering of metal targets made of In/Sn alloy. RF sputtering deposition of TCO commenced in 1970 with oxidized targets. Vossen and Poliniak [ 3 ] pioneered RF diode sputtering for depositing TO and ITO. Despite challenges in reactive sputtering process control, TCO deposition via sputtering remains relevant due to its advantages, including a faster vacuum chamber deposition cycle and improved film properties control in the post-bake process.
In the 1980s, research into TCO deposition and investigations into various dopants in ZnO began to emerge. By the late 1990s, p-type, like CuAlO 2 has been developed and showed good conductivity. Transparent p-n junctions were constructed using TCO materials. By 2000, the most recent developments in TCO technology, including theoretical insights and experimental proof of TCO conductivity, were hotly debated in the academic community. Meng et al. [ 4 ] reported on Mo-doped indium oxide (In 2 O 3 :Mo or IMO) as a high-performance TCO, demonstrating its superior characteristics with the lowest resistivity of 1.7 × 10 − 4 at low carrier concentration in 2 t o 4 × 10 20 c m − 3 and high electron mobility of 130 c m 2 / V − s on the glass substrate. Subsequently, other researchers explored IMO, experimenting with different deposition conditions and methods. They focused on increasing carrier concentrations by doping In 2 O 3 with Mo 6+ instead of Sn 4+ , rather than improving electron mobility.
A recent and significant trend in TCO development involves its application in flexible displays and electronics. The importance of thin-film mechanical properties and flexibility has led to extensive research on the mechanical characterization of ITO. The interest in flexible displays on temperature-sensitive plastic substrates has further prompted efforts to reduce resistance during ITO deposition, typically conducted at room temperature. However, the limited temperature tolerance of plastic substrates necessitates a process temperature well below the optimum for ITO, typically not exceeding 200 ° C . This aspect of flexible plastic substrates in TCO will be technologies that need transparent electrodes with better conductivity than liquid crystal display (LCD) and other E-field-based devices. Hence, there is a pressing need for enhanced TCO performance in these and future applications. ITO, the current standard, has two significant concerns: the price and the potential for a limited supply of indium, which is expensive and rare, underscore this need. Classical binary oxides (CdO, SnO 2 , In 2 O 3 , and ZnO) and Ga 2 O 3 with different dopants and combinations are possible for better TCO conductivity. Hence, the replacement of ITO with other materials will be discussed in Section 3.0 , where materials with perovskite structures are also considered in TCO development. ### 1.2. Recent research on transparent conductive oxides

However, transparent conducting oxides , such as ITO, FTO, and ZnO, uniquely combine electrical conductivity with high transparency. ITO has become the industry standard for transparent conductors in devices like LEDs, solar cells, displays, touchscreens, and smart windows. Although ITO offers benefits in conductivity, transparency, and environmental stability, its limitations include brittleness , low infrared transmittance, and incompatibility with plastic substrates, which restrict its use in next-generation flexible devices. Due to that, new transparent conductors are crucial to meet the requirements of emerging flexible electronics. These new materials must possess flexibility, work function control, improved morphology, low-cost production, and environmental sustainability. Over the past five years, these evolving requirements have driven renewed research into novel materials and processes.

## 2. Transparent conductive oxide


### 2.1. Characteristic

TCOs typically consist of metal oxides known for their high electrical conductivity and optical transparency, categorizing them as wide-bandgap oxide semiconductors with a bandgap greater than 3.2 eV. TCOs exhibit high optical transmission within the wavelength range of 400 to 700 nm, alongside electrical conductivity levels approaching that of metals. Often, additional elements are incorporated to enhance their metallic properties. TCOs effectively reflect infrared and ultraviolet wavelengths due to their narrow absorption bandgaps in the ultraviolet region, resulting in transparency to the human eye as they lack visible light absorption. These materials possess unique characteristics that are ideal for applications in electronics requiring optical access within electrical circuitry. A substrate classified as a TCO must exhibit low electrical resistivity, typically ranging from 10 − 3 Ω c m to 10 − 4 Ω c m . Furthermore, the film should demonstrate high optical transparency to visible light, with transmittance exceeding 80 % , owing to its wide bandgap of around 3.0 eV.
TCO layers are critical components in a wide range of photosensitive electronic devices, acting as transparent electrical contacts or electrodes in flat panel displays, touch screens, thin film solar cells, and electrochromic devices . However, only a few metal oxides doped with specific elements perform satisfactorily as TCOs. These include indium (In)-doped SnO 2 (ITO), fluorine (F)-doped SnO 2 (FTO), aluminum (Al)-doped ZnO (AZO), and gallium (Ga)-doped ZnO (GZO), each with its own set of limitations.
Recently, indium-based variants have dominated the TCO device market, characterized by a wide optical bandgap exceeding 3 eV, a resistivity as low as 10 − 5 Ω cm − 1 in the near-UV and visible spectrum, and an impressive transmittance of up to 90 % [ 37 , 38 ]. Transparent electrodes coated with Indium Tin Oxide (ITO) are extensively utilized in touch screens and electroluminescence lamps, with ITO-coated glass dominating a market valued at over 100 billion USD since 2006, while the smaller market for ITO on plastic film, estimated at around 100 million USD annually, is expected to grow substantially due to rising demand for mobile devices, flexible electronics, photovoltaic devices , and transparent electronics applications [ 21 ]. The multilayer structure of flexible perovskite TCOs significantly affects their quality and durability during bending. Mechanical defects such as cracks, delamination , and performance degradation will cause stress concentration at the interfaces between the substrate and functional layers of device efficiency . Flexible substrates of PI or mica are compatible to the mechanical properties that will reduce strain, while buffer layers and interface engineering improve mechanical durability [ 39 ]. Material of ITO and Al 2 O 3 nanoparticles will improves stress management, maintaining conductivity and transparency after extensive bending cycles. Application of ALD techniques for materials Al 2 O 3 are improve the efficiency, stability, and surface properties of perovskite solar cells [ 40 ]. The selection of flexible substrates with complementary mechanical properties can reduce strain which are minimizes stress concentrations and enhances the overall resilience of the device. Therefore, selection of materials and precise interface engineering are essential for ensuring the long-term stability and reliability of flexible perovskite TCO devices.

### 2.2. Optoelectronic properties


#### 2.2.1. Burstein-Moss shift

The semiconductor band theory explains the electrical properties found in TCO, which requires electrons to be stimulated over the bandgap from the valence band to the conduction band minimum ( CBM ) for electricity to conduct. The width of the bandgap determines the minimum energy required to excite an electron into conduction, with a broader bandgap, E g , greater than 3.0 eV, enhancing transparency to visible light by separating the maximums of the valence band ( VBM ) and the conduction band ( CBM ).
TCOs are materials infused with dopants to enhance charge carrier generation within their structure. The fundamental bandgap, representing the energy difference between E VB and E CB , is an inherent property of a material, while the optical bandgap is an extrinsic characteristic. The optical bandgap defines the lowest energy level that allows for an optical transition and determines a material's transparency, which is critical in TCO applications.
The injection of electrons from nearby defect donor levels directly into the conduction band results in n-type conductivity in TCOs, often created by defects like oxygen vacancies in metal oxide crystals. The electrons can disperse themselves from the sites of defects, thereby occupying electronic states at the CBM and causing a shift in the Fermi level above the CBM , if there is a sufficient degree of orbital overlap.

#### 2.2.3. Scattering mechanism

### 2.3. Alternative transparent conductive oxide

ITO is the most efficient and extensively adopted TCO material for electronic applications, primarily due to its exceptional optical transparency in the visible spectrum and meagre resistance. However, the demand for alternatives to ITO has surged, driven by the interest in flexible transparent conductive materials that offer ease of handling and low manufacturing costs [ 50 ]. In addition, interband absorption and light scattering by free electrons limit the use of ITO in ultraviolet optoelectronics due to its low transmittance in the ultraviolet region.
ITO is a solid solution compose of Tin (IV) oxide (SnO 2 ) and Indium (III) oxide (In 2 O 3 ), typically containing 10 % wt SnO 2 and 90 % wt In 2 O 3 by weight. Given that Indium is one of the rarest minerals found on Earth, its scarcity underscores the importance of optimizing its use in transparent conducting oxide . Nevertheless, indium is often found in its elemental form in natural environments, primary sourced from zinc minerals, iron, lead, and copper ores, and is commercially produced as a byproduct of zinc refining. Tin is mainly found in the ore cassiterite, which is a compound of tin (IV) oxide. The scarcity and high cost of indium in ITO, the most widely used TCO, have driven research and development efforts to find a replacement. Developing novel TCO materials requires meeting specific requirements such as low resistivity, low optical absorption, and cost-effective production.

### 2.4. Flexible transparent conductive oxide


#### 2.4.1. Material

Exciting outcomes have been accomplished on the stiff substrate, where high efficiency uses the hefty, inflexible, and delicate rigid substrate. The requirements of contemporary, flexible electronic devices are incompatible with rigid substrate due to its many disadvantages, including its weight, frangibility, and stiffness [ 79 ]. Due to the absence of these limitations, flexible solar cells have garnered considerable interest. A plastic substrate like PET is employed to realize flexible thin-film fabrication due to its cost-effectiveness, low energy consumption, robust mechanical properties, and suitability for continuous roll-to-roll fabrication techniques.
Compared to rigid glass devices, the difference in performance can be attributed to differences in active layer wetting on PET substrates [ 80 ]. Rigid devices had an efficiency of 22.1 % on rigid glass substrates but only 16.4 % on flexible glass ones because flexible glass is more expensive and brittle than rigid glass, which limits its use in many large-area applications and restricts its bending radius [ 81 ].
Flexible substrates are more applicable in the industry as compared to rigid substrates. Several performance characteristics of soft technology and wearable electronics strongly depend on substrate characteristics and influence mechanical stability. Flexible substrates are critical components for developing high-performance flexible electronics systems. Flexible polymers, rather than rigid glass, must be used as the substrate to meet the low-cost objectives of the emerging renewable energy and technology markets. Flexible substrates must generally enable rigid devices to perform well and have superior mechanical bendability and stretchability [ 82 ].
Stretchability is also critical for wearable electronic devices to meet the needs of various applications. However, rigid substrates have several disadvantages, including their weight, frangibility, and rigidity , which make them unsuitable for current, flexible electronic systems. Since it is easier to handle, transport, and manufacture with flexible TCO (lighter, easier to fracture, and less prone to manufacture), the search for an alternative ITO has intensified [ 83 ]. The solution is processable and has a low fabrication temperature ( ⟨ 150 ° C ), with a simple structure and high efficiency [ 82 ].

#### 2.4.2. Characteristic

Flexible substrates must meet several challenges to meet TCO requirements. Firstly, they must have good optical properties, meaning that light-absorbing devices must have visible light transmission rates of more than 90 %. Secondly, they must have high conductivity, meaning that charge-collecting layers directly relate to the fill factor and photocurrent density performance. They must have good mechanical properties, meaning that they have flexible substrates. Due to this, it calls for guarantees of dimensional stability, or the ability of materials to maintain their initial dimensions during processing or use. The chemical properties of the substrate have to be stable because it comes into contact with many chemicals during manufacture, like gases and solvents. Therefore, polymeric substrates, like PEN and PET films, resist various solvents. Then, physical barriers, such as exposure to air or water, can drastically reduce the functionality of electrical devices. The substrate's primary function is to act as a protective barrier, keeping out oxygen and moisture [ 82 , 84 ].
Several researchers have reported TCO flexible substrate performance for optoelectronic applications, which is mentioned in this paper review. Sharma et al. [ 67 ] stated that graphene could be considered as the best alternative to ITO for TCO because of its high flexibility, high conductivity, high transparency, and ease of process due to its excellent combination of sheet resistance and transparency. However, a limited production volume is the cause of the limited use of graphene. Besides, the initial material cost is high, which is also a drawback of graphene.
Dixon et al. [ 50 ] highlighted the difficulties in depositing polycrystalline ITO on flexible substrates like PET, which cannot endure temperatures beyond their glass transition temperature . In response, they developed a method using pulsed laser deposition (PLD) and radio frequency (RF) magnetron sputtering for producing low-temperature amorphous ITO films on PET substrates.
Sohn et al. [ 65 ] demonstrated that AZO can be successfully integrated as a TCO on a flexible polymer PES substrate for OLED applications, offering a viable alternative to address the limitations of traditional TCOs and ITO. However, these alternative materials must match ITO's transparency and conductivity while providing greater flexibility and durability as high temperatures required for producing polycrystalline ITO prevent deposition on flexible substrates, particularly polymers like PET, which cannot endure temperatures above their glass transition point. Bright et al. [ 21 ] highlighted the temperature limitations of common plastic substrates like PET, necessitating TCO process temperatures well below the optimal range for ITO, typically not exceeding 200 ° C . Consequently, flexible substrates have become integral to thin-film electronics and photovoltaics, gradually reducing the appeal of ITO in the realm of printable electronics.
Consequently, the prevailing choices for flexible substrates include ultrathin glass, metal foil, polymer films , and metal nanowire films. Metal nanowire films surpass ITO in flexibility and can even be engineered to be stretchable. Meanwhile, metal foil substrates like stainless steel and titanium boast superior mechanical properties and resistance to process chemicals, their poor light permeability restricts their utility as substrates for flexible solar cells. Glass substrates, with thicknesses of several hundred μ m , meet the criteria for flexible substrates outlined above, offering tolerance to temperatures exceeding 600 ° C , good conductivity, and chemical and dimensional stability. Nevertheless, their fragility, weightiness, and high cost stand out as significant drawbacks compared to other potential materials [ 85 ].

## 3. Perovskite

Calcium titanate is the main component of the mineral species known as perovskite, which comprises calcium titanium oxide. The perovskite structure exhibits flexibility and can manifest in various crystalline forms, including cubic, tetragonal, orthorhombic, trigonal, and monoclinic, contingent upon the specific A and B cations in ABO 3 . Consequently, both A and B components accommodate cations that can be readily substituted with suitable dopants at either A or B sites to enhance optoelectronic performance. Perovskite, first used to describe the oxide CaTiO 3 , is now widely used to describe oxides with ABO 3 structures and occasionally oxides with A 2 BO 4 structures.

### 3.2. Characteristic

Perovskite emerges as a promising candidate for optical applications, offering potential for developing advanced optoelectronic devices. With its inherent stability, diverse composition, and versatile characteristics, it presents a wide range of possibilities for applications [ 87 ]. Typically, the TCO devices must meet the requirements of high transparency, E g exceeding 3.0 e V for visible light, high conductivity, σ exceeding 10 3 S / c m , high carrier mobility , excellent long-term stability, and low cost [ 88 ]. Materials with perovskite structure have a large bandgap exceeding 3.0 e V , which enables transparency to wavelength greater than 400 nm, making it suitable for use as TCO [ 89 ]. Perovskite oxide, a structurally and chemically stable material, has recently shown strong physical properties as a new TCO. Researchers are interested in perovskite oxide because of its properties, which may make it appropriate for use as a TCO.
Perovskite materials have an ABO 3 crystal structure, where both A and B sites can host cations that may be doped to improve optoelectronic performance. A key challenge is growing thin-film layers with minimal grain and domain boundary densities [ 37 , 87 , 89 , 90 ]. Perovskite structures vary from simple ABO 3 or A 2 BO 4 to more complex configurations like A 2 B’O 6 , which is a double perovskite and A 2 A’B 2 B’O 9 , which is a triple perovskite, with MgSiO 3 and FeSiO 3 being among the most common. This review focuses on the use of perovskite structures as TCOs, particularly the typical ABO 3 crystallized structure. These materials belong to an important class with exceptional physical properties, including superconductivity , colossal magnetoresistance , ferromagnetism , piezoelectricity , and photoelectrochemical sensitivity. Due to pseudocubic crystal structure and compatible lattice, it can be used to fabricate devices with perovskite-type heteroepitaxial layers [ 37 ]. The simple and flexible structures of perovskite oxides allow for ion substitution, carrier addition, and precise oxygen incorporation.

### 3.3. Perovskite-type oxide as a transparent conductive oxide


#### 3.3.1. Alkaline earth metal vanadium oxide, AVO 3

The vanadium oxides of alkaline earth metals, including CaVO 3 , SrVO 3 and BaVO 3 , exhibit a V 4 + 3 d 1 electronic configuration, with a single electron occupying the partially filled t 2 g band. First-principle calculations show three t 2 g bands straddling the Fermi energy between − 1.5 eV to 1.5 eV [ 90 ].
According to Zhang et al. [ 48 ], SrVO 3 and CaVO 3 as correlated metal compounds, have the potential to serve as transparent conductors due to their excellent carrier concentration exceeding 2.2 × 10 22 cm − 3 , which is significantly higher than the carrier concentration of ITO, around 10 21 cm − 3 . As illustrated in Figure S1 , SrVO 3 exhibits cubic symmetry with a lattice parameter of Å 3.842 Å , which closely aligns with its electronic structure near the fermi level. The crystal field created by oxygen splits the vanadium's five 3d orbitals, with the valence band originating from the oxygen 2p orbitals. This splitting results in a higher-lying doublet state (e g ) and a lower-lying triplet state (t 2g ), the latter of which forms the conduction band.
He et al. [ 90 ] stated that SrVO 3 exhibits cubic symmetry with a lattice parameter of 3.842 Å where one V atom is surrounded by six O atoms in an octahedral configuration. Due to this, there is a strong correlation among metal oxides, and SrVO 3 is a promising transparent conductive material.
Xu et al. [ 91 ] identified that SrVO 3 emerged as a novel and promising TCO electrode. Films of SrVO 3 grown on rigid substrates exhibited notable properties, including a carrier concentration ranging from 2.0 to 2.6 × 10 22 1 / cm 3 , a carrier mobility between 5 to 10 cm 2 / Vs and an average transmission exceeding 60 % across the visible spectrum. Thus, SrVO 3 is a viable alternative to standard ITO films for various electronic device applications.
Zhang et al. [ 48 ] reported that SrVO 3 is an excellent transparent conductor because of its impressive electrical properties. Unlike typical wide bandgap semiconductors that need heavy doping to perform well, SrVO 3 stands out. The electrical conductivity, σ is given by σ = μ n e , which can also be defined by the equation σ = n m ∗ τ e 2 . Here, σ represents the electrical conductivity, μ denotes the carrier mobility, n indicates the carrier concentration, e stands for the electron charge, τ is the scattering time, and m ∗ is the electron effective mass. This relationship uncovers a significant advantage of SrVO 3 and CaVO 3 over traditional conductors. They exhibit higher conductivity and significantly lower plasma energy than epi-ITO, with a value below 1.75 eV. This unique combination allows them to achieve a superior balance between n , m , and τ, a level of performance that traditional conductors, lacking solid electron correlation, cannot match. These materials exemplify ideal transparent conductors. Enhancing the performance of transparent conducting materials involves maximizing electrical conductivity and electron charge and, increasing the carrier mobility and the effective electron mass ratio improves electrical conductivity while maintaining the free carrier reflection edge below the visible spectrum for clarity.

#### 3.3.2. Alkaline earth stannate, ASnO 3

The alkaline earth stannates such asbarium stannate (BaSnO 3 ), calcium stannate (CaSnO 3 ), and strontium stannate (SrSnO 3 ) stand out with their broad bandgap of about 3.0 eV , offering exceptional transparency for photovoltaic applications. Moreover, their electrical capabilities are remarkable, with room-temperature mobility soaring up to 320 cm 2 / Vs [ 90 ]. Lately, perovskite-structured alkaline earth stannates have been grabbing attention for their versatility in capacitors, solar cells, humidity and gas sensors, lithium batteries , and photocatalysts . Among them, BaSnO 3 , CaSnO 3 and SrSnO 3 with their distinct properties, are prime candidates for TCO applications. As Honorio et al. [ 92 ] assert, the quintessential perovskite structure is cubic, featuring the A cation nestled at the corners of the cube, each in 12 -fold coordination, while the B cation resides at the cube's center, encircled by oxygen anions, forming BO 6 octahedra (Refer to Figure S2 ).
The A cation in the ABO 3 structure includes compounds like BaSnO 3 , CaSnO 3 and SrSnO 3 . BaSnO 3 exhibits excellent TCO properties and high electron mobility at room temperature. Flaws in the structure of BaSnO 3 transform it from a perfect cubic perovskite to an n-type semiconductor with a bandgap of about 3.1 eV , enabling its use as a photocatalyst.
CaSnO 3 can be synthesized through a solid-state reaction between CaSnO 3 and SnO 2 , but a more cost-effective and lower-temperature alternative is the Pechini-polymeric precursor method. This approach reduces costs but also simplifies the production process. CaSnO 3 holds promise for various applications, including flat panel displays, water photoelectrolysis systems, and anti-static coatings. As an outstanding semiconductor with notable dielectric properties, CaSnO 3 is poised for numerous technological advancements.
SrSnO 3 boasts a wide bandgap and exceptional electron conductivity, making it an ideal candidate for TCO applications. Its deformed cubic structure characterizes this material, where the coordination around Sn 4+ remains consistent. The three-dimensional network of octahedra goes through interesting phase changes. As the temperature rises or dopants are added, the network changes from orthorhombic to tetragonal and tetragonal to cubic. These unique structural transformations enhance its functionality and broaden its potential uses.

Perovskite structures of the ABO 3 type exhibit a rich array of properties including ferroelectricity, superconductivity, and magnetoresistance , making them compelling subjects for exploration in photoelectrocatalytic applications. Among the vast assortment of ABO 3 perovskite compositions, strontium titanate (SrTiO 3 ) stands as a well-explored semiconductor, revered for its chemical robustness across different environments, notable photo responsiveness, and favorable band alignment, rendering it indispensable for multifaceted applications [ 93 ].
SrTiO 3 is a renowned oxide for its remarkable stability and optoelectronic characteristics, finding applications across various fields. However, the limited availability of rapid and scalable techniques for fabricating robust films at lower temperatures constrains its utilization in photoelectrocatalytic devices. Renowned as a paradigmatic functional perovskite oxide, SrTiO 3 is widely a substrate material for oxide thin film growth , boasting a broad bandgap and facile doping capabilities on cationic sites or oxygen vacancies . Extensive studies have showcased the heavy doping of SrTiO 3 with elements like La or Nb in both bulk and thin film forms, thereby attaining metallic conductivities [ 94 ].
SrTiO 3 features a typical perovskite structure, with the Sr cation coordinated by 12 oxygen anions and the Ti cation by 6 oxygen anions, primarily consisting of corner-sharing TiO 6 octahedra and the Sr cation occupying the central position. #### 3.4.2. Substrate

Substrate selection for TCOs influences their electrical performance, optical transparency, and mechanical durability. Glass and silicon oxide (SiO 2 ) substrates are preferred for rigid applications due to their excellent transparency and stability. SiO 2 as a protective barrier prevents ion migration and enhances device longevity, as reported in work Dou B. et al. [ 108 ], which explores the perovskite solar cells with improved moisture resistance on SiO 2 -coated substrates. Ultrathin polycrystalline silicon (poly-Si) substrates have good electrical conductivity and are suitable for advanced applications such as tandem solar cells and microelectronics. This substrate provides high charge carrier mobility and stability. In flexible applications, polyethylene terephthalate (PET) and polyethylene naphthalate (PEN) substrates are widely used because of their lightweight, flexibility, and compatibility with low-temperature processing. Zhang T. et al. [ 109 ] reported about flexible perovskite devices that achieve performance by using PET substrates and maintain their mechanical integrity during bending cycles. The PI substrate is suitable for demanding applications because of their high thermal and mechanical resistance. Polymer substrates like PMMA are integrated as interlayers to improve device efficiency , with hybrid tin halide perovskite solar cells achieving enhanced performance from 6.5 % to 10 % through PMMA application [ 110 ]. Substrate selection between rigid and flexible substrates is used, as protective coatings or interlayers which are essential for optimizing the performance, durability, and long-term reliability of perovskite TCO-based devices.
Perovskite layers and substrates having an affinity are crucial for maintaining the mechanical stability of flexible perovskite TCOs during bending stresses with strong adhesion in reducing the risk of cracks and delamination and maintaining the device performance. Perovskite solar cells on ultrathin flexible substrates retained 98.1 % efficiency at a 2 mm bending radius, highlighting robust adhesion and flexibility [ 111 ]. ITO-coated flexible substrates maintain at high efficiency after 1000 bending cycles, while mica substrates with van der Waals heteroepitaxy demonstrate exceptional durability properties that preserve conductivity and transparency even after 100 000 cycles [ 32 , 112 ]. Optimizing the interface between perovskite and substrate will enhance flexibility and long-term stability. Mica stands out among other flexible materials for its versatility, transparency, and affordability. In the world of flexible electronics, nothing beats mica, which is also flexible and durable. Its widespread use in crafting flexible devices is due to its unique flexibility blend, transparency for superior light transmission, and resilience to high temperatures.
In material innovation, there is a captivating focus on crafting flexible, functional oxide thin films by delicately growing them on mica substrates. Ma et al. [ 113 ] unveiled a technique for fabricating transparent, flexible, conducting oxide heteroepitaxial films by growing epitaxial molybdenum-doped indium oxide (IMO) thin films directly on a transparent, flexible muscovite mica substrate. These IMO films exhibit an average optical transmittance of 81.8 % in the visible spectrum and a mobility of 109 cm 2 V s . They achieve a figure of merit of 0.0976 Ω and a resistivity of 4.5 × 10 − 5 Ω cm , demonstrating mica's capability to provide excellent electrical and optical performance while withstanding mechanical stress. Nasiri et al. [ 114 ] revealed muscovite mica's potential as a pliable stage for transparent conductive AZO thin films cast through the fiery art of spray pyrolysis . Though predominantly polycrystalline, these films donned a hexagonal charm, boasting a transparency exceeding 80 % across visible and near-infrared domains while maintaining a resistivity of 10 − 3 Ω cm .
Ke et al. [ 115 ] demonstrated using transparent ITO electrodes on muscovite mica to create flexible optoelectronic devices that endure high temperatures. Thanks to mica's unique layered structure, the ITO/mica combination maintains low electric resistivity even after withstanding 1 , 000 continuous bends. This versatile material also functions as a transparent and flexible heater, reaching temperatures over 438 ° C in under 15 seconds. Additionally, when employed as a transparent electrode, ITO/mica facilitates the efficient production of flexible perovskite solar cells (PSCs), which can endure temperatures up to 450 ° C .

Most of these advanced technologies depend on transparent electrodes for effective operation. Successful commercialization necessitates the sustainable production of these transparent electrodes from readily available raw materials and ensuring their recyclability. Below are reports by various researchers highlighting the potential of TCO materials in the optoelectronic device technology market.
Afre et al. [ 38 ] stated that using TCO materials and producing transparent electrodes is beneficial for optoelectronic devices, resulting in multi-billion USD economy heavily reliant on the availability of ITO. Ye et al. [ 54 ] demonstrated that reducing the cost transparent conductor films lead to more affordable touch screens, solar cells and OLEDs. They optimized metal nanowire-based transparent electrode, which was the first alternative to ITO capable of being coated from solution. This advancement would enable optoelectronic performance that surpasses ITO. While the vapor-phase sputtering process adds to the production cost of a highly conductive 10 o h m s q ITO film, a high-performance, solution-coatable alternative could significantly lower the cost of producing transparent, highly conductive films.
Baraton [ 124 ] identified carbon nanotubes as promising alternatives due to their strength and flexibility. They can also be engineered as p-type conductors, which is significant given that the market for transparent conductors in touchscreen displays was projected to approach USD 400 million by 2015. In 2015, the advancement in nanoparticles and nanowires in inks and pastes were set to revolutionize the market. ITO inks and pastes were projected to reach the commercial stage, with sales approaching USD 600 million. At the same time, a range of nanostructures , including nanodots , nanorods , nanoneedles , nanowires, and nanoplates, was expected to generate over than USD 750 million in revenue with these nano-engineered transparent conductors. By 2014, the impact of nano-engineered materials was palpable, with revenues of USD 331 million from ITO substitutes. This promising trajectory has led many companies to believe nanomaterials will eventually outperform ITO.
Lewis et al. [ 125 ] reported that the extensive use of TCOs in rapidly growing consumer markets has intensified the demand for indium, which is crucial for producing FTO used in flat panel display. China has become a significant online supplier of indium, delivering 20 to 30 metric tonnes over the past two years. By recycling the unsputtered portion of ITO targets, China has successfully Japanese market demands and is positioned to maintain a stable supply. Historically, price volatility often resulted from sudden surged in demand, leading to short-term price increases. However, with ITO evolving into a commodity product for device manufacturers, alternatives made from less expensive raw materials could address these supply and cost issues. Therefore, researchers have placed greater emphasis on developing low-cost, lightweight, and mechanically flexible thin films that make it possible to implement portable, reduced-dimension roll-to-roll fabrication devices on a large scale.

## 5. Industrial consideration in optoelectronic technologies

Industrial applications in optoelectronics devices of flexible solar cells, displays, and wearable devices have a higher potential in flexible perovskite TCOs. Roll-to-roll printing and APPJ techniques are key industrial considerations for selecting flexible substrates and ensuring material stability through doping and encapsulation. Bending cycles of more than 10 , 000 are required to maintain mechanical durability without significant performance loss, whereas for longevity moisture barriers and UV protection are crucial. Non-toxic materials and recyclable components are used as sustainable practices to enhance further commercial viability. The flexible perovskite TCOs market has succeeded in driving industry standards and cost-effectiveness compliance. Due to this, researchers Seong S. et al. [ 34 ] and Zhao E. et al. [ 126 ] discussed enhancing the mechanical durability and optoelectronic properties of flexible PSCs. Seong S. et al. teams worked out on the strain-induced effects by using grain boundary reduction and interface engineering, while Zhao E. et al. teams worked on improved interface quality with Al 2 O 3 nanoparticles, and achieved a PCE of 23.83 % and 80 % efficiency retention after 5000 bending cycles. Their works are highlighting the importance of mitigating mechanical stress for the durable and reliable of flexible PSCs. The following is a review list of other researchers who reported on the development of flexible substrates for TCO applications based on industrial considerations.
Sohn et al. [ 65 ] delved into applying flexible polymer substrates to influence ITO thin films' mechanical stability for flexible OLEDs (FOLEDs). FOLEDs that use plastic substrates have gained attention for their ability to conform to various shapes, bend, or even roll up making them suitable for applications such as automotive windshields or aircraft cockpits. In cyclic bending tests however, the electrode layer, composed of inorganic materials like ITO and metal cathodes, often showed surface cracking due to the brittleness of these inorganic materials. Mechanical stress led to the formation of microcracks and the propagation of existing pinholes, which significantly impaired the contact properties between the organic and electrode layers.
The properties of FOLEDs, including current density and brightness, were examined across various bending tests. Several factors contributed to the diminished performance of bent FOLEDs. The increased driving voltage and reduced luminance observed in bent FOLEDs were primarily due to the heightened resistance of ITO after bending tests with a radius below 16 mm . Additionally, the properties of the interface between the organic and electrode layers played a significant role. OLEDs with a smooth ITO anode exhibited better device performance, including lower turn-on voltage and higher luminescent efficiency, due to improved contact properties. However, when fabricating FOLEDs with an ITO anode, performance deteriorated as the bending radius decreased to 16 mm . This decline was further exacerbated by increase sheet resistance in bent ITO, along with crack formation and surface roughness in both the ITO and Al inorganic layers.
Bright [ 21 ] explores the complex realm of industrial TCO deposition methods on plastic substrates, focusing on evaporation and sputtering techniques. Thermal evaporation, a widely used method in industries, is notable for its straightforward vacuum deposition . It is often applied to metalizing paper and plastics for food packaging and labelling. However, the challenges that can arise in TCO deposition using thermal evaporation, like those encountered in metal films, underscore the importance of our work in managing these obstacles. Another notable method is electron beam (e-beam) evaporation, which tackles substrate heating issues associated with radiation from hot evaporation sources. Challenges persist during evaporation despite its effectiveness in evaporating metals and metal oxides. TCO materials like ITO often exhibit oxygen deficiency, high carrier concentration, low mobility, and very low visible transmittance. Nevertheless, using e-beam, especially with ion assistance for enhanced film microstructure, holds promising benefits. However, pulse laser deposition (PLD) is only sometimes considered practical for large-area coating applications, such as wide rolls of plastic film, due to its higher cost associated with achieving a large deposition area. Moreover, the reduced incident laser energy density in PLD may lead to the deposition of TCO with inferior properties. Thus, while each method boasts its merits and limitations, understanding their nuances is imperative for effective TCO deposition on plastic substrates in industrial settings.
Multipoint techniques have been demonstrated in commercially available PLD systems, and multisource methods, such as e-beam sources, are also feasible. Sputtering occurs when gas ions bombard the target material's ejection from plasma. An applied electric field attracts the ions towards the target. In sputtering applications, a DC electric field is employed, and magnets are positioned behind the magnetron sputtering target to capture electrons, thereby enhancing ion bombardment of the target, which leads to an increased deposition rate and reduced substrate heating. The DC magnetron sputtering process typically operates at an argon pressure ranging around 1 to 10 μ ( mTorr ) , or 0.13 Pa to 1.3 Pa . One notable aspect of sputtering, which contributes to improved microstructure, is that the atomic composition of the metallic-deposited film matches that of the target material. Creating an ITO film with a 90 % InO 2 O 3 / 10 % SnO 2 ( wt . % ) composition or using a metal alloy target with a 90 % In / 10 % Sn ( wt . % ) composition results in ITO with the correct mixture ratio at the atomic level. The magnetron sputtering process can be implemented in various configurations, including direct current (DC), alternating current (AC), mid-frequency (MF), and radio frequency (RF). These configurations can be deployed in various formats, including cylindrical, inverse cylindrical, rotatable, and dual magnetron formats. Among these, DC magnetron sputtering stands out as the primary commercial techniques for depositing TCO films such as ITO. This method utilizes a metal target in a reactive process or may employ a ceramic target, whether conductive or insulating, to produce the TCO.
Magnetron sputtering sources present numerous advantages for thin film deposition . Firstly, they offer scalability in length, facilitating large-scale substrate coating, a crucial requirement in various industries. Secondly, they are well-suited for temperature-sensitive substrates, including plastic materials, owing to their relatively gentle deposition process. Additionally, magnetron sputtering can operate at lower pressure levels compared to diode sputtering, enhancing process efficiency and reducing the risk of substrate damage. Finally, the deposition film's atomic composition closely matches that of the target material, ensuring high-quality coatings. Across industries, magnetron sputtering is found to be extensively used in coating thin metal and metal oxide films onto glass and plastic substrates, some exceeding widths of 4 m. This is particularly prevalent in producing energy-efficient architectural windows, where precise deposition techniques are essential for optimal performance and durability.
Researchers from ‘Telviv University’ [ 74 ] discussed the industrial applications of TCO coatings on transparent materials, particularly for work surfaces and closet doors, highlighting their utility in clean rooms for electronics assembly to prevent static charges and in transparent heating systems for defrosters in aircraft and vehicular windshields. TCO coating offers several advantages, including faster and more uniform defrosting over large areas than traditional hot air blowers. While applied to passenger vehicles have proven technically successful, they have faced commercial challenges due to the high cost of supplemental alternators required to deliver the necessary high voltage. This has led to their use in shielding applications to reduce electromagnetic radiation interference (EMI) while allowing visual access. TCO coatings have found extensive applications in flat panel displays, solar cells, and architectural coatings. In LCD technology, TCO films serve as electrodes, allowing backlighting to pass through the liquid crystal film while voltage is applied to individual pixels, with ITO commonly chosen for this application due to its electro-optical properties and ease of acid etching. In solar cell applications, TCO films serve as transparent electrodes, meeting conductivity and transparency requirements while considering stability under environmental conditions. Tin oxide-based films are preferred for their environmental stability. In architectural glass, TCO coatings are part of multi-layer stacks in window applications to help minimize air conditioning costs in the summer and heating costs in the winter for buildings equipped with appropriately coated windows.
The emergence of new applications has sparked a surge in the utilization of TCO materials, particularly for fabricating transparent electrodes in optoelectronics, driven by the search for alternatives to traditional materials like ITO. This trend has led to a concerted effort to explore the properties, physics and chemistry of TCO materials. Research and development have focused on understanding the electrical conductivity and optical transparency of TCO thin films, with the goal of achieving conductivity levels comparable to or exceeding those of ITO. Despite significant advancements, TCO semiconductors with conductivity matching or surpassing that of ITO are still elusive. Recent progress in developing ternary, quaternary, and binary-combination TCOs shows promise, through their conductivity often falls short of ITO, highlighting the ongoing pursuit of novel TCOs with superior performance characteristics.
Bright [ 11 ] highlighted the key applications of TCO coatings, focusing on two major examples: magnetron-sputtered ITO-coated glass as transparent electrodes in flat-panel displays (FPDs) and pyrolytically deposited SnO 2 : F on float glass as a low-emissivity (low-e) coatings in energy-efficient windows. ITO's use in FPDs constitutes the largest annual market for TCOs in terms of dollar value, while low-e coatings represent the most extensive deposited area of TCOs each year. The diverse requirements of these applications drive the need for enhanced TCO performance. For instance, ITO is increasingly patterned into finer lines for next generation display transparent electrodes to achieve higher conductivity and reduce electrical losses. Additionally, emerging technologies such as OLEDs demand even greater conductivity in transparent electrodes compared to LCDs and other E-field based devices. The growing demand for solar PV devices and the expanding energy generation market led to increased TCO consumption for transparent electrodes. Low electrical losses and high conductivity in transparent electrodes are necessary. In 2 O 3 ( IMO ) and In 2 O 3 : Ti ( ITiO ) are desirable choices due to their ability to apply high electron mobility to TCO. They exhibit superior properties to ITO, with higher transmittance and high conductivity in the near-infrared region.
The discussion focuses on future applications aimed at improving the conductivity of ITO, which currently stands at around 1.2 × 10 − 4 Ω cm which represents the best reproducible resistivity compared to a reported value of 1.77 × 10 − 4 Ω cm by Fraser and Cook [ 127 ]. In theoretical first-principle methods, certain limitations are identified. These limitations include challenges related to ionized impurity scattering, with an efficiency of around 90 cm 2 V 2 , and the need to maintain a maximum carrier concentration of 2 × 10 21 c m 3 while ensuring that the plasma wavelength remains outside the visible spectrum of 800 nm . Consequently, ITO is expected to maintain around resistivity of 4 × 10 − 5 Ω cm . These limitations apply to ITO and other traditional metal-doped TCOs like ZnO:Al and SnO 2 :Sb. However, they do not apply to TCO materials doped with transition metals. While there is a possibility of further technological advancements to reduce resistivity, it is essential to note that predicting a tenfold (10x) improvement is highly uncertain. In the next decade, there will likely be a maximum twofold reduction in ITO resistivity on glass.
Lewis et al. [ 125 ] highlighted the swift expansion of flat-panel display (FPD) technology, where TCO applications were gaining momentum. In this landscape, ITO emerged as the material of choice across diverse industries, including aerospace, architectural glass, solar energy, and lighting. Each sector harnessed ITO with tailored property combinations to suit their specific needs. Novel applications for FPDs, especially those involving the deposition of ITO onto heat-sensitive polymer substrates or polymer-based color filters, introduce distinct challenges. This application necessitates low substrate temperatures and, in certain cases, low-kinetic-energy deposition methods. Additionally, TCOs are increasingly utilized for electromagnetic shielding in cathode-ray tubes for video display terminals. This application involves applying a water-based ITO powder suspension through various technologies such as sputtering, polymer lamination, and spin-coating.
Furthermore, TCOs versatile applications in advanced glazing for windows, ranging from passive thermal management in architectural, automotive, and aircraft window glass to more advanced electrically activated structures, exemplified by electrochromic (EC) windows. In this evolving field, the versatility and adaptability of TCO materials are continuously expanding, influencing the future of multiple industries.
ITO functions as a transparent conducting electrode (TCE) in this application, orchestrating a fascinating transformation where a small current flows through it, causing the initially transparent tungsten oxide to adopt a deep blue hue, indicating a shift its trioxide oxidation state. This is particularly relevant in the realm of cutting-edge, lightweight, flexible display technologies, where the deposition of high-performance ITO or alternative transparent conductor onto heat-sensitive polymer substrates is essential. Achieving optimal ITO microstructures is crucial for these polymer-based substrates in FPD applications, as they ensure low resistivity and enhanced transmissivity.

In this ever-evolving landscape, innovation thrives at the intersection of materials science, engineering, and creativity.

## 6. Challenges and advantages in optoelectronic technologies

In this century, optoelectronics researchers have focused on improving performance and altering the mechanical properties of materials, primarily by enhancing deformability under external stress conditions like squeezing, bending, or stretching. These advancements in mechanics and fabrication have significantly improved the performance of flexible electronics. In addition to traditional metal materials, carbon-based materials and conductive polymers serve as excellent alternatives to transparent ITO electrodes due to their superior flexibility. Furthermore, research on flexible TCO is ongoing, as different products require varying conductivity levels, flexibility, and optical transmittance. Challenges in the TCO field include their application in wearable products and solar cells, where the absence of uniform for testing and evaluation standards for flexibility poses a significant issue. Future research should address additional properties of TCO materials, such as stability, degradability, and cost-effectiveness. Additionally, advancing the industry necessitates improvement in the production process, encompassing material synthesis through to device manufacturing.
Other researchers have a few challenges and advantages in their work on flexible perovskite as TCO, as listed below. Liang et al. However, the rigidity of ITO could induce cracks in thin film, resulting in device failure after repeated bending. They utilized polymer-based interfacial layers and low-cost carbon and organic composite materials, addressing the cracking issues.
Ren et al. [ 79 ] significantly enhanced the performance of flexible PSCs by employing low-temperature fabrication methods for high-efficiency metal-oxide electron and hole transport materials. They also improved PEDOT:PSS conductivity through modification and strengthened the mechanical properties of flexible substrates. These advancements led to the highest reported power conversion efficiency (PCE) for flexible PSCs, reaching an impressive 18.9 % . This notable achievement was largely facilitated using a PET/ITO substrate, underscoring the effectiveness of their innovative material selection.
Ye et al. [ 128 ] delve into recent strides in flexibility, stability, and large-scale production of flexible PSCs, underscoring their potential as a game-changer in wearable technology. Wang et al. [ 129 ] conducted a thorough review of recent advancements in flexible PSCs, focusing on efficiency, reliability, and cost. They investigated low-temperature processed interlayers and electrode materials to boost efficiency, leading to enhanced device performance. The study suggested using new flexible electrodes instead of traditional conductive metal oxide to address reliability issues, thereby extending the lifespan of flexible PSCs. Additionally, the review highlighted the continuous manufacturing process for producing these cells as a cost-effective method. With efficiencies reaching 15.3 % , this approach shows promise for commercialization, offering opportunities for continuous improvement in device performance while maintaining high yields.

## 7. Conclusion

Currently, ITO is the dominant TCO material for organic optoelectronic devices. However, the escalating cost of ITO, driven by the limited availability of indium and rising market needs, conflicts with the affordability goal of organic devices. Thus, identifying alternatives to ITO is critical for making OPVs and OLEDs more cost-effective. While various emerging materials have been explored as potential TCOs, not all offer better performance than ITO.
Perovskite structures are one alternative to ITO, with their intriguing physical properties and cost efficiency. Early transition metal-based perovskite oxides with partially filled d orbitals have been extensively studied and utilized as novel TCOs. Replacement of the substrate with a flexible substrate is gaining great interest due to its lightness, flexibility, and transparency. Various techniques have been used on several flexible substrates, such as polyimide, polycarbonate , polyethene terephthalate, and polyethene naphthalene for the deposition of ZnO and AZO thin films. However, there are some restrictions for polymer substrates. Moreover, thermal stress between the substrate and thin-film is significant. Although glass substrate and metal foils could overcome the mentioned limitations, they are rigid and cannot be applied to flexible devices. PLD deposited a perovskite TCO film onto a transparent and flexible mica substrate, demonstrating its potential as a cost-effective and structurally compatible TCO electrode. This advancement enables the creation the creation of transparent, flexible, and self-powered hybrid organic-organic perovskite photodetectors. The perovskite TCO exhibited outstanding optical transparency and electrical conductivity even after 10 5 bending cycles, positioning it as a promising candidate for commercial applications and advancing the development of advanced, transparent, flexible, and wearable optoelectronic devices.
Flexible perovskite TCOs is significant in material science, like ITO where address the brittleness and cost limitation of traditional TCOs. The need for flexible optoelectronic devices that can endure bending and deformation includes applications like wearable sensors, rollable displays, and flexible solar cells. Perovskite TCOs promise enhanced performance through high conductivity, transparency, and chemical versatility as an alternative. The potential for a cost-efficient and scalable manufacturing process makes perovskite TCOs a valuable alternative to ITO in flexible solar cells, enabling lightweight and portable power sources, in addition to OLED displays and wearable sensors. Due to this, optimize the conductivity and transparency are developing a scalable fabrication technique, where its remains a challenge in ensure a long-term stability under mechanical stress and environmental conditions. Nowadays, research is increasingly focused on explore a new perovskite composition, that improve the fabrication methods, and investigate the mechanical properties in enhance the durability. The potential of flexible perovskite TCOs is driving the evolution of optoelectronic technology toward more adaptable and efficient devices.
