
## 1. Introduction

Silicon heterojunction (SHJ) solar cells have achieved high efficiencies exceeding 27 %, driven by their superior surface passivation and low-temperature processing compatibility. With the development of SHJ solar cells, In 2 O 3 -based transparent conductive oxides (TCO) usually deposited through magnetron sputtering or reactive plasma deposition , have been essential for large-scale production [ [1] , [2] , [3] , [4] ]. However, the scarcity of indium resources, combined with the fluctuations of its price and the supply risks influenced by geopolitical factors, has affected its large-scale application and promotion [ 5 , 6 ]. With global indium reserves projected to deplete within decades under current consumption trends, the development of indium-free or indium-lean TCOs has become a critical imperative for advancing green energy technologies and achieving carbon neutrality goals [ 7 ].
There are many studies on In-lean/In-free TCO and TCO-free window designs. Zinc oxide (ZnO) and tin oxide (SnO 2 ) are effective alternative n-type semiconductor materials for the next generation TCO which have excellent optical transmittance and electrical performances [ [8] , [9] , [10] ]. The chemical composition, band structure, oxygen vacancy number, and crystallinity of TCO have important effects on optical and electrical properties. The doping of B, Ga, and other impurities could modulate the photoelectric property of ZnO thin films . Mandal et al. [ 11 ] have achieved the modulation of surface morphology and optical and electrical properties of BZO films by varying the concentration ratio of different gases; as a result, the optimized film reached a resistivity of 6.51 × 10 −4 Ω cm with a mobility of 19.05 cm 2 /V·s and a sheet resistance of 7.23 Ω/sq. Ramakrishna Reddy et al. [ 12 ]studied the effect of doping concentration and different substrate temperatures on the properties of GZO films, grown with a doping concentration of 5.0 at% at 350 °C. The GZO film has a grain size of about 98 nm, a conductivity of 1.32 × 10 3 Ω −1 cm −1 , and a transmittance of more than 85 % in the visible range. Meng et al. [ 13 ] obtained a 23.65 % efficiency of SHJ cells using GZO films prepared by RPD. The average transmittance of the GZO films grown at an oxygen flow rate of 60 sccm was 85.35 % in the wavelength range of 390–780 nm, while the resistivity decreased to 6.24 × 10 −4 Ω cm when the oxygen flow rate was reduced to 10 sccm. The SnO 2 films commonly are n-type semiconductors, characterized by optical transmittance as well as chemical and thermal stability. Koida et al. [ 14 ] deposited a-SnO 2 films utilizing SnO 2 ceramic tablets as an evaporation source by reactive plasma deposition (RPD), in which highly ionized particles provided by arc discharge have lower energies (in the range of 10–40 eV) than those in magnetron sputtering (MS) (>100 eV) and the bombardment damage to the TCO layer by energetic ions can be well reduced. Yu et al. [ 15 ]used MS technology to prepare undoped amorphous SnO x (a-SnO x ) with carrier mobility of 22 cm 2 V −1 s −1 and 2.38 × 10 −3 Ω cm. By partially replacing the indium-based TCO layer with the undoped a-SnO x film, the efficiency of the SHJ solar cell reached 25.91 %. However, magnetron-sputtered TCO thin films often introduce plasma-induced interfacial defects during sputtering deposition and generates high-energy ions that damage ultrathin a-Si:H (or described as a-Si) layers, reducing minority carrier lifetimes and open-circuit voltage ( V O C ). Qiu et al. showed that TCO sputtering damage on the contact layer in SHJ solar cells with hydrogenated nc-Si:H is due to ion bombardment during sputtering and can't be fully recovered by low-temperature annealing [ 16 ]. Therefore, it is great significance to develop In-lean/In-free transparent conductive layers with low damage and adjustable work function. In this work, we developed RPD-grown Zn x Sn 1-x O transparent conductive films and their application as a buffer layer in SHJ solar cells. The microstructure and photoelectric properties of the Zn x Sn 1-x O films were systematically studied. Zn x Sn 1-x O is an In-free material and its work function can be adjusted by changing the ratio of zinc to tin. As a buffer layer , it can reduce interface defects caused by ion bombardment , effectively resist damage caused by sputtering. It can also improve the energy level matching with n-a-Si:H and carrier transport. Finally, the photovoltaic conversion efficiency (PCE) of the SHJ solar cell reached 20.76 %.

## 2. Experimental section


### 2.1. Zn x Sn 1-x O thin film growth

Zn x Sn 1-x O thin films were deposited on 1.1-mm-thick Corning Eagle XG glass substrates using a reactive plasma deposition (RPD) system under room temperature. Prior to deposition, substrates underwent sequential 15 min ultrasonic cleaning in acetone and ethanol, followed by thorough deionized water rinsing. The concentrations of targets are 0, 30, 50, 70 and 100 wt% SnO 2 -doped ZnO (purity 99.99 %), respectively.
An 80 sccm flow rate of argon was passed through the electron gun during deposition, and an additional 30 sccm flow rate of argon was passed into the chamber. The deposition pressure was maintained at 0.5 Pa, and the power was set at 1800 W. The substrate temperature was monitored by thermocouples and remained below 50.0 °C during all sample deposition processes , with the increase in substrate temperature mainly due to partial electron beam surface treatment. GZO thin films deposition is the same as the Zn x Sn 1-x O. The experimental conditions for sputtering system are as follows: the vacuum was less than 5 × 10 −4 Pa. The distance between the target and the substrate was maintained at 190 mm. The substrate temperature was 150 °C, and the power was 70 W. The In 2 O 3 : CeO 2 (3.0 wt %) target was utilized, and the sputtering gas was argon.

### 2.2. Fabrication of SHJ solar cells

Double-sided intrinsic a-Si:H passivation layers were grown on n-type crystalline silicon (n-c-Si) wafers serving as the absorber in SHJ solar cells. The device architecture employed a rear-junction configuration with p-a-Si:H emitter layers deposited on the rear side and n-a-Si:H layers on the front side via plasma-enhanced chemical vapor deposition (PECVD). Transparent conductive oxide (TCO) films were subsequently deposited using reactive plasma deposition (RPD) and magnetron sputtering, creating four distinct front-side configurations: 110 nm-ICO monolayer; ICO/20 nm-Zn 0.17 Sn 0.83 O bilayer; ICO/30 nm-Zn 0.17 Sn 0.83 O bilayer; and ICO/50 nm-Zn 0.17 Sn 0.83 O bilayer. Rear-side electrodes comprised either 110 nm-ICO or 545 nm -GZO films. The Zn 0.17 Sn 0.83 O buffer layer was positioned between ICO and n-a-Si:H layers, as depicted in Fig. 1 . We used a thermal evaporation equipment to grow fully-coverage Ag rear contacts and front grid lines. The spacing of the evaporation electrodes was 1 mm, the cell area was 0.7 cm 2 .

## 3. Results and discussion


### 3.1. Microstructure analysis

Zn x Sn 1-x O films with different doping concentrations were deposited on 1.1 mm-thick Corning Eagle XG glass using the RPD technique at room temperature. Fig. 2 (a) presents XRD patterns of RPD-deposited Zn x Sn 1-x O thin films, with reference spectra of pristine SnO 2 and ZnO for comparative analysis. Distinct diffraction peaks emerge exclusively in pure ZnO and Zn 0.81 Sn 0.19 O films, while other compositions exhibit featureless patterns indicative of amorphous structures. The sharp (002) diffraction peak in pure ZnO corresponds to hexagonal wurtzite phase crystallization, moreover ZnO typically crystallizes in the hexagonal wurtzite structure, characterized by tetrahedral coordination of Zn 2+ and O 2− ions with lattice parameters a = 3.25 Å and c = 5.21 Å. The Zn 0.81 Sn 0.19 O spectrum retains this characteristic (002) orientation, albeit with reduced intensity, confirming partial wurtzite structure preservation. In contrast, SnO 2 presents a tetragonal rutile structure, where Sn 4+ ions are octahedrally coordinated by six O 2− ions, yielding larger lattice constants ( a = 4.74 Å, c = 3.19 Å). The structural mismatch between the wurtzite and rutile phases inherently limits crystal formation in Zn x Sn 1-x O systems, particularly under nonequilibrium deposition conditions . Fig. 2 (a) reveals progressive amorphization with increasing SnO 2 incorporation. Crystalline-to-amorphous transition initiates when Sn/Zn exceeds 0.5 among Zn x Sn 1-x O system, forming SnO 2 -dominated matrices. This structural transformation aligns with ionic radius disparity between Sn 4+ (0.69 Å) and Zn 2+ (0.74 Å), where Sn substitution induces lattice distortion and defect formation in ZnO frameworks. Sn doping amplifies these crystallographic defects , ultimately degrading long-range order and promoting amorphous phase formation.

### 3.2. Optical and electrical properties

To further verify the impact of structure and composition evolutions, optical transmittance and absorption of Zn x Sn 1-x O films on glass substrate have been measured as shown in Fig. 4 (a) and (b). Fig. 4 (a) displays transmission spectra of Zn x Sn 1-x O films with varying Zn x Sn 1-x O compositions. All Zn x Sn 1-x O films exhibit high visible-range transmittances, which improves with increasing Sn content . Notably, the absorption edge shifts toward shorter wavelengths (blue shift) as SnO 2 concentration rises, indicating bandgap widening. In order to further analyze the reasons for the wide spectral range of the Zn x Sn 1-x O film, the plasma wavelength was calculated based on the Drude model. The relationship formula is as follows: λ ρ = 2 π c ( ε ∞ ε 0 m ∗ n e 2 ) 1 / 2 where λ ρ represents the plasma wavelength, c is the speed of light, ε 0 is the vacuum dielectric constant, ε ∞ is the high-frequency dielectric constant, m∗ is the effective mass of free electrons , and n is the carrier concentration, with e being the electron charge. According to the Drude theory, electromagnetic waves with frequencies lower than the plasma wavelength have strong reflection. Therefore, the plasma wavelength determines the cutoff wavelength of the film in the near-infrared region. The plasma wavelengths of all the Zn x Sn 1-x O films are greater than 4.0 μm. The free carriers absorption is not significant, and the transmittance in the near-infrared region remains unchanged. It demonstrates that a carrier concentration of 1.0 × 10 20 /cm 3 does not exhibit significant free carrier absorption, and the transmittance in the near-infrared region stay unaffected [ 30 ].

Fig. 4 (d) shows the simplified band structure diagram of the Zn x Sn 1-x O thin film, where the work function was measured by ultraviolet photoelectron spectroscopy (UPS). For Zn x Sn 1-x O films, the work function varies significantly with the Sn content. The work function of Zn 0.65 Sn 0.35 O reaches the maximum of 4.76 eV. Before reaching the maximum work function, the work function of Zn x Sn 1-x O increases with the increase in Sn content, and the Fermi level ( E F ) of Zn x Sn 1-x O decreases. What's more, this trend inversely correlates with carrier concentration data in Table 4 , where Zn 0.65 Sn 0.35 O exhibits minimal carrier concentration (2.15 × 10 15 cm −3 ). After reaching the maximum work function, the work function of ZTO decreases with the increase in Sn content, and the work function of Zn 0.17 Sn 0.83 O is the minimum, with a higher carrier concentration. It should be noted that the work functions of the zinc-rich region and the Sn-rich region may be similar to those of ZnO (4.38 eV) and SnO 2 (4.32 eV).
The resistivity, carrier concentration, and Hall mobility of Zn x Sn 1-x O thin films prepared with varying Zn x Sn 1-x O compositions were systematically investigated. Table 4 summarizes the resistivity results of the synthesized Zn x Sn 1-x O films. A composition-dependent trend is evident: the resistivity initially increases with rising SnO 2 concentration, peaks at the Zn 0.65 Sn 0.35 O composition, and subsequently decreases with further SnO 2 incorporation. Although the resistivity of SnO 2 and ZnO is superior to that of Zn x Sn 1-x O, the focus of this work is to control the work function of the transparent conductive layer (Zn x Sn 1-x O) between TCO and a-Si:H, and to adjust the energy band structure to achieve a buffer effect between the existing TCO and a-Si:H. Therefore, one should not only consider the resistivity but also the matching of the work function.
The resistivity (ρ) of Zn x Sn 1-x O thin films is governed by the relation: ρ = 1 σ = 1 n q μ where σ denotes conductivity, n is carrier concentration, q is elementary charge, and μ represents carrier mobility . In Zn x Sn 1-x O systems, Sn atoms diffuse into the ZnO matrix , creating interstitial lattice vacancies. These vacancies enhance valence electron mobility , thereby reducing resistivity (or increasing conductivity) as derived from the above equation. Sn atoms, acting as donors due to their lower electronegativity compared to Zn, elevate carrier concentrations through substitutional doping.
Oxygen vacancies ( V o . . ) critically influence electronic properties via the defect equilibrium: O o x = 1 / 2 O 2 ( g ) + V o . . + 2 e −
This reaction generates doubly charged oxygen vacancies and free electrons, significantly boosting carrier density. As shown in Table 4 , Zn 0.17 Sn 0.83 O exhibits optimal electrical performance with minimal resistivity (5.57 × 10 −3 Ω cm), highest carrier concentration (4.01 × 10 19 cm −3 ), and improved mobility (27 cm 2 V −1 s −1 ). Consequently, Zn 0.17 Sn 0.83 O was selected as the buffer layer for SHJ solar cell integration to leverage its superior charge transport characteristics.

### 3.3. Zn x Sn 1-x O as transparent conductive layers in the SHJ solar cells

The electrical contact between metals and semiconductors can be classified into two types: Schottky contacts and Ohmic contacts , determined by the work function difference between the transparent conductive oxide (TCO) and amorphous silicon (n-a-Si:H). Consequently, Zn 0.17 Sn 0.83 O with work function alignment ( W F = 4.32 eV) near to n-a-Si:H is strategically selected as the buffer layer. The buffer layer has the characteristic of reducing contact resistance and promoting the effective transport of charge carriers. For example, Junfeng Zhao et al. analyzed the work function and contact resistance of a gradient Zn (O, S)/Mg electron-selective contact for dopant-free silicon solar cells , demonstrating that the low work function of Mg (3.6 eV) and gradient Zn (O, S) deposition reduced contact resistance to 35.89 mΩ cm 2 , enabling a device efficiency of 20.92 % [ 33 ].

To verify the stability of the devices, the humidity and heat test was conducted in an atmosphere environment with a temperature of 85 °C and a relative humidity of 85 %. After 15 h, the device efficiency decreases from 20.76 % to 20.19 %. Using the MS and RPD equipment, 10-min ICO, GZO and Zn 0.17 Sn 0.83 O films are grown on a 10 × 10 cm glass. It is found that the difference in resistance is within 5 % and the difference in thickness is within 3 %. Magnetron sputtering can easily achieve large-area uniformity and can be applied to industry. Generally, for the RPD thin film growth experiments in the laboratory, only one plasma gun is used. To extend its application to the industrial sector, multiple plasma guns can be employed to ensure uniformity over large areas.

## 4. Conclusions

In summary, we have systematically studied the structural, optical, and electrical properties of Zn x Sn 1-x O thin films grown by using RPD technology with reduced bombardment. Through compositional tuning of Zn x Sn 1-x O, we achieves optimized optoelectronic properties: a low resistivity (5.57 × 10 −3 Ω cm for Zn 0.17 Sn 0.83 O), a high carrier mobility (27 cm 2 /V·s), and a tunable bandgaps (3.19–3.96 eV). Notably, Zn 0.17 Sn 0.83 O exhibits a work function of 4.32 eV, similar to the work function of n-type amorphous silicon. The integration of a 30 nm-Zn 0.17 Sn 0.83 O buffer layer between magnetron-sputtered ICO and n-a-Si:H preserves minority carrier lifetime (1437.99 μs), enabling SHJ solar cells to achieve a champion efficiency of 20.76 % ( Voc = 730.48 mV, Jsc = 40.19 mA/cm 2 , FF = 70.51 %). This work provides inspirations for further optimizing performance of Zn x Sn 1-x O materials. In this way, the use of ZnxSn1-xO thin film can improve the performances of SHJ solar cells by better meeting the electrical and optical requirements.
