## Abstract

With the increasing diversification of application environments of new electronic products, demands are being placed on flexibility, transparency, and stable operation capability even under harsh conditions of electromagnetic shielding films. Aiming at the issue of high contact resistance of simple silver nanowire (AgNWs) conducting network, this work utilizes orthogonally arranged AgNWs and graphene oxide (GO) to establish unique AgNWs/GO conductive network, achieving more conductive nodes and enhancing the stability of the conductive network. The resulting AgNWs/GO film exhibits a reduced resistance of 9.8 Ω/sq, an impressive light transmittance of 85.7 % (λ = 550 nm), and excellent electromagnetic interference shielding efficiency (EMI SE) reaching 30.3 dB in the X-band (8.2–12.4 GHz). Moreover, the AgNWs/GO film exhibits the potential for long-term operation in harsh environments. After undergoing numerous bending cycles (10,000 times), prolonged exposure (90 days), and repeated energization (800 times), the resistance of the AgNWs/GO film remains extremely stable. Furthermore, the film demonstrates remarkable electrothermal conversion capability in cold environments, rapidly heating to 73.3 ℃ under a 4 V voltage and maintaining stability thereafter. After 800 h of operation, there is negligible change in its electrothermal properties. The development of such a flexible electromagnetic shielding film holds significant implications for various applications.

## 1. Introduction

In recent years, amidst the rapid advancement of science and technology, a plethora of electronic devices have emerged, ushering in a significant impact of electromagnetic waves on daily life. Consequently, there has been an escalating demand for electromagnetic shielding materials that possess the qualities of being lightweight, readily producible at scale, highly transparent, enduring, and effective across diverse environmental settings [1] , [2] , [3] , [4] , [5] , [6] , [7] . Undoubtedly, this presents a formidable challenge. Traditionally, Indium Tin Oxide (ITO) has been esteemed for its optimal balance of transparency and conductivity. However, its intrinsic mechanical fragility renders it inadequate for potential applications in flexible electronics [8] , [9] , [10] . Subsequently, one-dimensional materials like AgNWs and carbon nanotubes (CNTs), coupled with two-dimensional materials such as graphene and transition metal carbide/carbon-nitride (Mxene) sheets, have been employed as conductive additives to cater to various demands [11] , [12] , [13] , [14] , [15] , [16] , [17] . The electromagnetic shielding efficacy of a material correlates directly with its electrical conductivity and permeability [18] . To fabricate a flexible transparent film endowed with superior electromagnetic shielding properties, it is imperative to establish a stable and excellent conductive network through reasonable structural design. Wang et al. employed CNTs as conductive additives to establish a conductive network characterized by numerous nodes, producing a flexible conductive material imbued with electromagnetic wave (EMW) shielding capabilities, achieving an EMI SE of 30.7 dB [15] . This inspires us that constructing more conductive nodes is an effective approach to enhance electromagnetic shielding performance.
Among numerous conductive fillers, one-dimensional flexible metal nanowires stand out for their high aspect ratio, which is advantageous for constructing conductive networks [12] , [19] . These networks hold the promise of significantly enhancing the shielding performance of materials and hold tremendous potential in manufacturing transparent electromagnetic shielding films. Particularly, AgNWs stand out due to their exceptional conductivity, significant aspect ratio, and minimal permeability threshold [11] , [13] , [20] . Consequently, AgNWs are widely utilized as conductive additives, with researchers predominantly employing spin-coating [21] , [22] , [23] and rod-coating [24] , [25] , [26] techniques to fabricate electromagnetic shielding films containing AgNWs. Some studies have employed chemical soldering or amalgamation with other substances to augment the electromagnetic shielding performance of these films [27] , [28] , [29] . Unfortunately, most of the AgNWs obtained by the above-mentioned method are randomly distributed. In fact, the arrangement of fillers will affect the formation of conductive nodes, thereby influencing the final electromagnetic shielding performance of the film [30] . Furthermore, the durability of single AgNWs films is limited. This is not only because AgNWs exposed to air are prone to oxidation, resulting in decreased electromagnetic shielding performance, but also because the shear and peeling stresses generated at the interface between the AgNWs conductive network and the substrate during bending will restrict its flexibility. Therefore, designing more optimal arrangements of AgNWs, while synergistically constructing a more stable composite conductive network with other conductive fillers, provides a new pathway for enhancing the preparation of excellent electromagnetic shielding films [17] , [31] , [32] , [33] , [34] .
To construct a composite conductive network, we focus our attention on two-dimensional carbon nanomaterial fillers with excellent conductivity and flexibility [35] , [36] , [37] . The combination of one-dimensional AgNWs with two-dimensional carbon nanomaterials generates a synergistic effect, significantly enhancing the electromagnetic shielding performance of the material. Among numerous carbon nanomaterials, the flaky GO emerges as a promising nanofiller due to its ultra-high aspect ratio, minimal permeability threshold, excellent electrical properties, and outstanding electromagnetic shielding efficiency [35] . If one-dimensional AgNWs and two-dimensional GO are co-dispersed, GO serves as a bridge and protector, enhancing the stability of the conductive network and reducing the resistance of the film. Considering the inherent opacity of GO, it is necessary to regulate the content of GO, minimizing its impact on the optical performance of the film, and achieving a balance between electromagnetic shielding performance and optical performance. Based on this, further construction of composite conductive networks with AgNWs arranged in different patterns is carried out to determine the optimal arrangement for building more conductive nodes. Our research will provide theoretical guidance for the development of multifunctional flexible and transparent electromagnetic shielding films tailored for complex environments.

## 2. Experimental section


### 2.1. Experimental materials

AgNWs were dispersed in water with line length of 35–40 μm, line diameter of ∼ 60 nm, and L/D ratio of ∼ 600 from Nanjing Xianfeng Nanomaterials Science and Technology Co. Ltd. GO was purchased from Shenzhen Suiheng Science and Technology Co. Ltd. anhydrous ethanol was purchased from Shanghai Sinopharm Chemical Reagent, and the deionized water was homemade in the laboratory, and all of the above reagents were of analytically pure grade, and were not subjected to any purification treatment.

### 2.2. Preparation of AgNWs/GO thin films


#### 2.2.1. Preparation of GO dispersion

We weighed 100 g of ethanol and 1 g of GO, and after half an hour of mechanical stirring, put them into an ultrasonic cleaner for 2 h, during which time the temperature of the ultrasonic cleaner was set to 30 °C. The GO dispersion was obtained, and the concentration of the dispersion was 7.8 mg/ml.

#### 2.2.2. Preparation of AgNWs/GO thin films

For the preparation of an orthogonally aligned AgNWs network, the conductive ink was dispensed drop by drop onto the ultrasonically cleaned PET film to form a long, slender, and even ink line. A Mayer bar was then employed to draw the AgNWs ink through the straight line at a consistent speed, thereby forming a uniform thin liquid film. This ink was applied onto the PET film at a coating speed of 8 mm/s, ensuring the deposition of all AgNWs onto the PET substrate. Subsequently, the film coated with AgNWs ink underwent thermal annealing at 100 °C for 7 min. The PET substrate underwent another coating perpendicular to the pre-aligned direction, with the aforementioned steps repeated to obtain an AgNWs film featuring orthogonal alignment. Similarly, to achieve an isotropically distributed AgNWs network, repeat the above steps, but with the direction of coating on the PET substrate being the same for both the front and back coatings. A randomly arranged AgNWs films will serve as the control group. For this purpose, a minute quantity of AgNWs conductive ink was meticulously added dropwise onto the PET film to ensure uniform coverage, and the desired film was obtained after annealing under the same conditions.
Subsequently, the prepared GO dispersion was uniformly sprayed onto the aforementioned films using a commercial spray gun (Pistole AFC-101A). The films were then subjected to annealing at 130 °C for 2 min, yielding films with a GO density of 0.62 mg/cm 2 . The procedure was repeated to produce AgNWs/GO films with varying GO densities of 0.62, 1.24, 1.87, 2.49, and 3.12 mg/cm 2 , respectively. The pure GO film can be obtained by spraying the GO dispersion directly on the PET film surface under the same spraying conditions, and its surface density is 0.62 mg/cm 2 .
We numbered the sample as AgNWs-X /GO-Y, where X is o, u, or r, respectively, representing the AgNWs network is orthogonally arranged, uniformly arranged, and randomly distributed; Y is 1,2,3,4,5, respectively, representing the surface density of GO on the film is 0.62, 1.24, 1.87, 2.49, 3.12 mg/cm 2 .

### 2.3. Characterisation

The distribution and combination of AgNWs and GO in AgNWs/GO films and pure AgNWs films were analyzed and observed using a Leica microscope (DM750P) and a scanning electron microscope (SU9000); the resistance of AgNWs/GO films was tested using a four-probe resistivity tester (HPS2661), and the resistance of the films was recorded at different bending levels using a Tektronix DMM 6500 multimeter. To avoid change in the results, each sample was measured 7 times, the maximum and minimum values were discarded and then averaged; infrared images were taken using an infrared thermal imaging camera (FOTRIC 288); the light transmittance of the films was tested using a haze tester (SGW-810); and the transparent conductive films of AgNWs/GO were cut into rectangles of 22.6 mm × 10.2 mm. The films were tested based on the waveguide method using a vector network analyzer to test the EMI SE of the samples in the frequency range of 8.2–12.4 GHz based on the waveguide method, using the measured scattering parameters (S 11 and S 21 ) to calculate the power coefficients and EMI SE with the following formulae: (1) R = S 11 2 (2) T = S 21 2 (3) A + R + T = 1 (4) S E R = - 10 log 1 - R (5) S E A = - 10 log ( T 1 - R ) (6) S E T = S E R + S E A of these, the A, R, and T are the reflection, absorption, and transmission coefficients, respectively. SE R , SE A , and SE T are the reflection efficiency, absorption efficiency, and total EMI efficiency, respectively.

### 3.1. Effect of AgNWs arrangement on properties of films

The optical microscope images of the three types of AgNWs networks are depicted in Fig. 1 (b-d), to express the orientation of AgNWs more precisely, we utilize the Fast Fourier Transform (FFT) function within Image J software. To enhance the performance of the conductive network, the two-dimensional GO is combined with the existing AgNWs network to form a new binary conductive network. GO is sprayed onto the films featuring the previously fabricated AgNWs networks, whether randomly arranged, isotropically distributed, or orthogonally aligned, resulting in AgNWs/GO films with a surface density of 0.62 mg/cm 2 . As shown in Fig. 1 (e-g), a black substance covering the network is composed of GO ( Fig. S1 ). Compared to the simple mechanical blending method, the step-by-step layering sapproach uniformly covers GO on the AgNWs network ( Fig. S2 ). The FFT images depicted in the upper right corner of Fig. 1 (e-g) also reveal that the introduction of GO does not exert any influence on the arrangement of AgNWs. Upon measuring their resistances, it is observed that the resistance values decreased to 20.7, 16.1, and 13.7 Ω/sq, respectively, indicating reductions of 2.5, 2.3, and 2.3 Ω/sq, as shown in Fig. 1 (h). This may be due to potential poor contacts between the pure one-dimensional AgNWs conductive network, leading to localized high resistance. Upon the addition of the two-dimensional GO, GO grows and covers the AgNWs network, bridging the loosely connected parts and forming a more tightly connected conductive network with increased conductive nodes. Compared with pure AgNWs, the presence of GO enhances the connectivity and completeness of the conductive network, thereby improving the electrical and electromagnetic shielding properties of the film. Further comparison indicates that the reduction effect of GO on the resistance of conductive networks with different arrangements is similar. Light transmission is also a crucial property for flexible films, impacting their applicability in specific scenarios. As shown in Fig. 1 (h) and the optical image in Fig. 1 (i), the light transmission of films featuring orthogonally and isotropically arranged AgNWs is superior to those with randomly arranged AgNWs. This phenomenon arises from the non-uniform distribution of conductive fillers in randomly arranged AgNWs films, leading to the concentration of AgNWs in certain areas and consequent poor light transmission. Additionally, the addition of GO contributes to a decrease in light transmission due to the inherent opacity of GO. Furthermore, upon adding GO, the inherent opacity of GO leads to a slight reduction in the optical transmittance of the film, yet it still remains above 85.7 %.

### 3.2. Effect of the surface density of GO on properties of films

After conducting analysis and comparison, it is confirmed that GO indeed alters the film properties. To assess the impact of varying GO content on the conductive network and the resulting changes in film properties, we spray GO dispersion onto the prepared AgNWs films, obtaining AgNWs/GO films with GO surface densities of 0.62, 1.24, 1.87, 1.49, 3.12, and 3.12 mg/cm 2 , respectively. The addition of GO significantly reduce the resistance of the film. As depicted in Fig. 2 (a), with increasing GO surface density, the film resistance gradually decreases from 16 Ω/sq to 9.8 Ω/sq. This reduction can be attributed to the gradual coverage of the AgNWs network by flaky GO particles, resulting in less uncovered area and a more perfected GO layer, thereby optimizing the conductive network. We also investigate the transparency of AgNWs/GO films closely related to their application scenarios. With the increase of GO surface densities, the transmittance of AgNWs/GO film decreases from 96 % to 85.7 %, as illustrated in Fig. 2 (b). This reduction is attributed to the inherent opacity of GO itself. However, the film's transmittance remains above 85 %, thereby meeting the requirements of the vast majority of scenarios. In summary, upon comparing AgNWs/GO films with different mixing and arrangement methods, it is evident that the orthogonally arranged AgNWs/GO network exhibited the best performance.

### 3.4. Electromagnetic shielding properties of films

The electromagnetic shielding performance of a substance is indeed positively correlated with its electrical conductivity, with two key factors influencing the construction of the conductive network: the chosen material and the type of AgNWs network constructed. The EMI SE curves in the X-band and the corresponding average value of SE R , SE A, and SE T for pure AgNWs with different arrangements and AgNWs/GO films with an added surface density of 0.62 mg/cm 2 of GO are shown in Fig. 4 (a-b). It is evident that films exhibit the best EMI shielding performance when the AgNWs are arranged in an orthogonal manner (24 dB), followed by an isotropic distribution (21 dB), and the least effective performance is observed when they are randomly arranged (15 dB). This phenomenon can be attributed to the relationship between EMI SE and the orientation of the AgNWs. As the orientation improves, AgNWs are more uniformly distributed. Consequently, when electromagnetic waves penetrate the conductive network, fewer waves escape due to local loopholes. Furthermore, compared to isotropic distribution, the orthogonal network features a greater number of conductive junctions, resulting in superior EMI SE performance. After the addition of GO, its adherence to the conductive network significantly impacts the electromagnetic shielding performance of the films. The EMI SE of the film is increased by 2.8, 3, and 3 dB respectively. This enhancement can be attributed to the synergistic formation of a more closely integrated conductive network by AgNWs and GO, thus improving the EMI shielding performance of film both in SE R and SE A .

This increase is attributed to the positive correlation between SE R , SE A , and the perfection of the conductive network [18] , [42] , [43] . As the GO content increases, the defects of the conductive network are continuously addressed, and the conductive network acts as a carrier for dissipating mobile charge carriers, leading to an increase in SE A [44] . SE R depends on the interaction between mobile charge carriers and incident microwaves, and the number of mobile charge carriers increases with the increase in GO content [44] .
The electromagnetic shielding principle of the AgNWs/GO film is elucidated in Fig. 5 . Upon arrival of the incident electromagnetic wave at the AgNWs/GO film, the substantial impedance mismatch between the film and air interfaces causes most of the EMW to be reflected [43] . Given that air is non-conductive, the higher the conductivity of the non-magnetic material, the greater the difference in conductivity with air, resulting in higher reflectivity. The penetrating EMWs interact with the high electron densities of AgNW that contributes to ohmic losses, leading to an energy loss of EMWs. The unconsumed EMW will reflect back and forth in the conductive network until it is dissipated or absorbed as heat or other forms of energy. Such reflection exists both between AgNWs and between AgNWs and GO in different layers. When AgNWs are orthogonally arranged, the number of conductive nodes is large and the division is uniform, and the efficiency of reflecting dissipative EMW is the highest. Compared with the pure AgNWs network, after adding GO, the two substances form a better conductive network, improved electron transport, and impeded the escape of the incident EMW, thus improving the electromagnetic shielding effect. By examining Eqs. (1–6), it can be seen that for the AgNWs-O film, A = 0.136, T = 0.0039, R = 0.8601; and the AgNWs-O/GO-5 film, A = 0.0687, T = 0.0009, R = 0.9304. The decrease in the value of A and the increase in the value of R indicates that most of the electromagnetic wave energy is reflected, far exceeding the amount absorbed [42] . It shows that the film is still the main means of electromagnetic shielding by reflecting electromagnetic waves.

### 3.5. The reliability of thin films against external forces

The reliability of EMI shielding films against external forces is another crucial aspect of shielding materials in modern flexible optoelectronic systems [45] . To assess the reliability of the AgNWs/GO films against external forces, we bend both the AgNWs-o films and AgNWs-o/GO-2 films into various arcs, measuring their resistance change rates. The resistance change rate of AgNWs-o/GO-2 film is only 2 % as the bending arcs range from 10 to 1 mm. In comparison, the AgNWs-o films exhibit a 31.2 % high resistance change rate, as shown in Fig. 6 (a). In specific environments, films may need to be bent multiple times, to assess fatigue behavior, AgNWs-o/GO-2 films were subjected to 10,000 bends. After 10,000 bends, the resistance of the AgNWs-o/GO-2 film increases by only 5 %, showing a slight increase. In contrast, the AgNWs-o film exhibits a resistance increase of 8 % after 1,000 bends and a substantial increase of 31 % after 10,000 bends, as shown in Fig. 6 (b). The phenomenon observed in the AgNWs-o film is attributed to the weak adhesion of AgNWs on the PET substrate. During bending, some AgNWs may detach from the overall conductive network, resulting in an increase in resistance. With multiple bends, the detachment of AgNWs increases, leading to a higher rate of resistance change. Additionally, the conductive junctions in the AgNWs network become fewer after multiple bends. In contrast, the AgNWs-o/GO-2 film, with the presence of GO, maintains a tightly bound conductive network. When bent, the AgNWs do not warp and peel off, resulting in a less pronounced drop in resistance, the microscopic mechanism diagram is shown in the Fig. 6 (c). Microscopic observations before and after 10,000 bends confirm the preservation of the AgNWs-o/GO-2 film's conductive network, as shown in Fig. 6 (d), while the density of AgNWs on the AgNWs-o film decreases. The addition of GO contributes to the enhanced adhesion and resilience of the conductive network, providing improved reliability against bending-induced stresses.
The reliability of EMI shielding films against external forces is another crucial aspect of shielding materials in modern flexible optoelectronic systems [45] . To assess the reliability of the AgNWs/GO films against external forces, we bend both the AgNWs-o films and AgNWs-o/GO-2 films into various arcs, measuring their resistance change rates. The resistance change rate of AgNWs-o/GO-2 film is only 2 % as the bending arcs range from 10 to 1 mm. In comparison, the AgNWs-o films exhibit a 31.2 % high resistance change rate, as shown in Fig. 6 (a). Flexible electromagnetic shielding films based on metal conductive networks face a challenge: over time, the metal conductive network undergoes oxidation, leading to a decline in electromagnetic shielding performance. To address this issue, a protective layer, such as GO, can be applied to the metal nanowires to shield the inner metal conductive network from oxidation. The AgNWs-o/GO-2 film and AgNWs-o film are subjected to 0–800 cycles of energization, with each cycle applying a voltage of 4 V for 1 h. The resistance of the films is tested every 10 cycles, and the recorded data are presented in Fig. 6 (e). As the number of energization cycles increases, the resistance change rate of the AgNWs-o/GO-2 film remains low at 1.5 %, while that of the AgNWs-o film reaches a significant 55 %. This result can be attributed to the effective coverage of the AgNWs network surface by GO, isolating it from the air and significantly slowing down the oxidative deterioration of AgNWs, thereby extending the lifespan of the electromagnetic shielding film. The AgNWs-o film and AgNWs-o/GO-2 film are left at room temperature for 0–90 days, and the resistance change rate was recorded daily, as illustrated in Fig. 6 (f). The resistance of the AgNWs-o film exhibits considerable variation with the number of days, reaching as high as 43 %. In contrast, the resistance of the AgNWs-o/GO-2 film change almost negligibly over the same duration, registering only 1 %. This phenomenon is attributed to the degradation of silver upon contact with oxygen, leading to the generation of silver oxide particles and consequent high resistance. The GO layer on the AgNWs-o/GO-2 film effectively shields the AgNWs, preventing their oxidation. Consequently, whether subjected to numerous energization cycles or extended periods, the resistance change in the AgNWs-o/GO-2 film is nearly imperceptible. The presence of GO significantly enhances the film's working stability and enduring effectiveness.

### 3.6. Electrothermal properties of thin films

Transparent and flexible electromagnetic films are commonly employed in diverse outdoor settings. Exposure to cold environments inevitably impacts the performance of electromagnetic shielding films. Therefore, these films should possess a degree of self-heating capability to counteract adverse conditions and maintain their electromagnetic shielding effectiveness [46] , [47] , [48] . Employing Joule's law ( Q = U 2 R t , where Q, U, R, and t denote the Joule heat generated by the film, bias voltage, resistance, and operating time, respectively), AgNWs-o/GO-2 films are anticipated to exhibit exceptional electro-thermal conversion due to their low sheet resistance. Cut the film to a size of 20 × 20 mm 2 , at distinct voltage levels and operating for 110 s, the temperature variations are illustrated in Fig. 7 (a). When the applied voltage ranges from 1-4 V, AgNWs-o/GO-2 films exhibit rapid heating and stabilize at specific temperatures (44.6–73.3 °C). Upon withdrawing the applied voltage from both ends of the film, the film temperature swiftly returns to room temperature, showcasing an exceptionally rapid thermal response. Furthermore, when a continuously varying voltage is applied to the film ends, the film's temperature response remains exceptionally swift and accurate, As depicted in Fig. 7 (d). Moreover, Joule's law closely aligns with the linear relationship between temperature and the square of the input voltage, as depicted in Fig. 7 (b), indicating the highly feasible manipulation of the film's temperature by adjusting the input voltage.

## 4. Conclusion

In summary, the orthogonally arranged AgNWs conducting network cooperated with GO to build an excellent AgNWs/GO conductive network with a low resistance of 9.8 Ω/sq, a transmittance of 85.7 %, and excellent electromagnetic shielding performance (30.3 dB) in the X-band. Moreover, After undergoing numerous bending cycles (10,000 times), prolonged exposure (90 days), and repeated energization (800 times), the resistance of the AgNWs/GO film remains extremely stable. Furthermore, the film demonstrates remarkable electrothermal conversion capability, rapidly heating to 73.3 ℃ under a 4 V voltage and maintaining stability thereafter. It shows that the film can be used in low-temperature environments. The AgNWs/GO film emerges as a highly promising and advanced transparent electromagnetic interference shielding material, particularly well-suited for deployment in challenging environmental conditions.
