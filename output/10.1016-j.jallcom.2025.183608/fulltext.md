
## 1. Introduction

Electrical conductivity is directly proportional to the density of free charge carriers. While enabling the flow of electric current, these free charge carriers typically absorb electromagnetic (EM) radiation. Therefore, metallic conductors are opaque to visible light. However, a special group of materials, transparent conductive oxides (TCOs), exhibits both EM transparency– particularly to visible light with wavelengths (λ) between 400–700 nm– and electrical conductivity. This dual functionality makes TCOs invaluable in many applications, including displays, photovoltaic cells, and light-emitting devices [1] , [2] , [3] . There are two approaches to achieve transparent conductivity in TCOs: extrinsic doping and multilayer structures. Extrinsic doping provides the required density of free charge carriers by substituting the metal in the oxide with another element of higher valency. Traditionally, TCOs have been dominated by tin-doped indium oxide (ITO). However, reliance on ITO leads to significant challenges due to the rarity and high cost of indium , as well as ITO's poor durability under ultraviolet (UV) irradiation. These issues have fueled a search for alternative materials [4] , [5] . Recent advancements involve using other metal oxides with bandgaps ( ) in the 3–4 eV range, thus offering the necessary transparency. The most notable of these alternatives are zinc oxide doped with gallium and aluminum [6] , [7] , [8] , as well as fluorine-doped tin oxide (FTO) [9] , [10] .
The second approach is to use an oxide/metal/oxide (OMO) multilayer. Research on OMO structures has shown great potential for developing TCO materials. Silver is especially well-suited as the metal of choice due to its high free-charge concentration and relative UV transparency resulting from its surface plasmon resonance at 320 nm (3.88 eV) [11] . Although the silver layer is confined as a middle layer, the entire OMO structure becomes conductive due to silver diffusion. The validity of this approach has been demonstrated using several oxides [12] , [13] , [14] , [15] , [16] , [17] , [18] , where the deposition rate, post-treatment, and layer architecture were shown to be effective for tuning the transparency–conductivity balance in TCOs.
Recent technological advances have increased the demand for TCOs in the UV range, 200 ≤ λ ≤ 400 nm, which are needed for various applications , including UV optoelectronics such as light-emitting diodes (LEDs), laser diodes, extreme ultraviolet (EUV) lithography, and space technologies [19] , [20] , [21] , [22] . Conventional TCOs are inefficient in this spectral range because of their relatively lower bandgap. Recently, different approaches have considered using wide bandgap (WBG) metal oxides with eV, which ensures transparency in both the visible and UV ranges. However, extrinsic doping of these materials is challenging [23] , [24] , [25] . Concurrently, OMO structures have shown great potential for developing TCO materials suitable for UV applications [26] , [27] , [28] , [29] , [30] , [31] .
To date, the most common WBG oxide used in OMO structures for UV TCOs has been gallium oxide ( ₂ ₃ ). Zhang et al. produced ₂ ₃ /Ag/ ₂ ₃ multilayered films at room temperature [32] . These films had a 95.15 % transmittance at 325 nm with a figure of merit (FOM) of 5.83 × 10⁻³ Ω⁻¹ . Wang et al. developed an ITO/Ag/ ₂ ₃ multilayer with a sheet resistance of 2.82 Ω/◻ and a transmittance of 86.7 % at 365 nm, thus significantly improving the light output power of AlGaN-based UV LEDs [33] . Additionally, Kim et al. studied ₂ ₃ /Ag/ ₂ ₃ multilayers showing an average optical transmittance of 87.16 % in the 300–350 nm wavelength range with a sheet resistance of 8.48 Ω/◻ and an FOM of 29.81 × 10⁻³ Ω⁻¹​​​​​​ [34] . Liang et al. optimized the ITO/ ₂ ₃ /Ag/ ₂ ₃ multilayer and achieved a transmittance of 86.4 % at 335 nm with a sheet resistance of 3.43 Ω/◻ [35] .
In this study, we examined OMO multilayer structures using five WBG metal oxides: erbium oxide ( ₂ ₃ ), ₂ ₃ , hafnium oxide ( ₂ ), tantalum pentoxide ( ₂ ₅ ), and zirconium oxide ( ₂ ). These materials have WBGs within a range of 4.0–6.0 eV and exhibit high transparency in the UV range [36] , [37] , [38] , [39] , [40] , [41] , [42] , [43] . The properties of these structures depend on the deposition techniques; therefore, metal oxides must first be systematically investigated to enable a meaningful comparison of their suitability as UV-TCOs. This study identified the most promising material that offers the best balance between transparency and electrical conductivity. The thickness of the Ag layer was optimized because it is crucial for enhancing both optical transmittance and electrical conductivity, thereby improving performance in UV optoelectronic applications.
The work was conducted in two stages. First, individual oxide layers were deposited to investigate their optical properties, surface morphology, and chemical composition. Subsequently, OMO structures were fabricated using different Ag thicknesses for each oxide. The optical and electrical characteristics of these structures were evaluated for their potential as UV-TCOs. The films were synthesized through physical vapor deposition (PVD): electron-beam (e-beam) evaporation for the metal oxides and thermal evaporation for Ag. PVD is a relatively simple technique that produces films with minimal contamination due to its vacuum environment. The unique features of this work include the development of TCOs with UV transparency and metal-like conductivity, room-temperature deposition, and the ability to control conductivity by adjusting the Ag layer thickness. This study developed transparent conductors in the deep UV region (λ < 300) – an area that is rarely investigated due to the challenges of extrinsic doping in WBG metal oxides.

## 2. Experimental details

The films were prepared using a Leybold L560 coater and deposited on unheated fused silica and molybdenum substrates. The purity of all materials was at least 99.9 %. Before deposition, the substrates underwent ultrasonic cleaning with methanol, isopropanol, and deionized water, followed by blow-drying with high-purity nitrogen gas. The deposition system was initially evacuated to a base pressure of 8 × 10 −5 mbar. Intentional substrate heating was avoided to prevent undesirable diffusion among the layers; however, unintentional heating (to no more than 80 °C) could not be completely prevented due to radiative heating from the evaporation sources. The substrates were rotated to ensure uniformity during deposition. The film thickness and deposition rate, maintained at 1 Å/s, were controlled using a quartz crystal thickness monitor. The deposition process had two stages. In the first stage, individual metal oxide thin films ( ₂ ₃ ₂ ₃ ₂ ₂ ₅ , and ₂ ) were deposited via e-beam evaporation of pellets or granules of the respective metal oxides. These layers, each 250 nm thick, were used to determine the fundamental properties of each metal oxide and served as a baseline for comparison with the OMO structures. In the second stage, five tri-layers (metal oxide/Ag/metal oxide) were fabricated for each metal oxide. For these multilayers, the metal oxide layers were deposited with a thickness of 45 nm while the silver layers ranged from 4 to 12 nm in 2-nm steps using thermal evaporation from a molybdenum boat. The total thickness of the OMO structure was . The multilayers were deposited sequentially under vacuum. A schematic of this process is shown in Fig. 1 .

The surface morphology of the films was examined with atomic force microscopy (AFM) using a Bruker Innova diSPM system. The chemical properties of the films were analyzed by X-ray photoelectron spectroscopy (XPS) for the metal oxide samples. These measurements were performed on a Thermo Scientific Escalab 250Xi spectrometer equipped with a monochromatic Al X-ray source and an instrumental energy resolution of 0.5 eV. To prevent surface charging, XPS measurements were done on samples deposited on molybdenum substrates. A field-emission scanning electron microscope (FESEM; Thermo Scientific Quattro S) was used to image the plan-view surfaces of the O/Ag/O multilayers. The bulk chemical composition of the OMO structures was analyzed using energy-dispersive spectroscopy (EDS) with an Oxford X-MAX N50 detector integrated into a ThermoFisher FEI Quanta FEG 250 scanning electron microscope. The EDS measurements used an electron beam energy of 10–15 keV and had an energy resolution of 5.9 keV. The electrical properties were evaluated using a four-probe geometry (van der Pauw) configuration with an Ecopia HMS 3000 Hall-effect measurement system. The transmittance spectra of the films at normal incidence were measured from 200 to 800 nm using a JASCO V-570 double-beam spectrophotometer.

## 3. Results and analysis


### 3.1. Structural and chemical properties

Structural analysis of the films was based on X-ray diffraction and revealed that all films were amorphous. This is due to two factors. First, the films, especially those in OMO structures, were ultrathin. Second, metal oxide thin films deposited on unheated substrates are typically amorphous because the deposited species lack the thermal energy needed for crystalline growth [44] . Amorphous thin films have smoother surfaces, which enable sharper interfaces between the layers in the OMO structures.
Typical three-dimensional AFM images of the multilayer structures are shown in Fig. 2 . These images reveal that the oxide films display a uniform columnar microstructure. The surface morphology was evaluated by the root-mean-square surface roughness ( ). The oxide films had values of 1.12 ± 0.25 nm; these values are only 0.5 % of the total thickness of the individual oxide layers, thus indicating that the surfaces are smooth. The surface roughness of the OMO structures ( Fig. 3 ) did not show a consistent trend with changes in the silver layer thickness. Ga 2 O 3 had a negligible change in surface roughness, although some fluctuations occurred. Conversely, Er 2 O 3 had a decrease in surface roughness as the silver layer thickness increased. The other three oxides (HfO 2 , Ta 2 O 5 , and ZrO 2 ) had an overall surface roughness that increased with the silver layer’s thickness with Ta 2 O 5 showing the highest roughness values. Nevertheless, apart from Ta 2 O 5 , the roughness values ranged between 0.5 nm and 1.5 nm. The growth of individual Ag layers was also investigated. AFM images of 4-nm- and 6-nm-thick Ag thin films ( Fig. 4 ) clearly illustrate the transition from island-like to continuous growth. A continuity threshold of approximately 6 nm was observed, which is notably lower than the typical threshold thickness for continuous Ag growth reported in the literature [45] , [46] , [47] , [48] , [49] , [50] , [51] , [52] . This reduction in threshold thickness is attributed to the relatively slow deposition rate employed here (1 Å/s). Both the deposition rate and deposition technique can strongly influence the morphology of the Ag films, thus affecting the observed growth mode.

The surface electronic properties, oxidation states, and chemical composition of the metal oxide thin films were examined using XPS. Wide-scan survey spectra confirm the successful deposition of thin films of ₂ ₃ ₂ ₃ ₂ ₂ ₅ and ₂ showing characteristic peaks for their respective metal cations and oxygen anions with minor contamination by carbon after exposure to ambient air. High-resolution core-level spectra ( Fig. 5 ) were used to analyze the stoichiometry and oxidation states of the metal oxide thin films. The Er 4 d spectrum has a broad peak centered at a binding energy (BE) of 169.2 eV, consistent with the Er 3+ oxidation state of ₂ ₃ [53] . The peak broadening aligns with the multiplet splitting observed in rare-earth oxides [54] . Similarly, the Ga 2 p spectrum peaks at 1118.1 eV (Ga 2 ) and 1145.0 eV (Ga 2 ) , which is characteristic of Ga 3+ in ₂ ₃ and agrees with published data [55] , [56] . The Hf 4 f spectrum features peaks at 16.8 eV (Hf 4 ) and 18.3 eV (Hf 4 ), corresponding to the Hf 4+ oxidation state and consistent with earlier reports [57] , [58] . Likewise, the Ta 4 f spectrum shows two distinct peaks at 26.3 eV (Ta 4 ) and 28.1 eV (Ta 4 ) indicative of Ta 5+ in Ta 2 O 5 [59] , [60] , [61] . The Zr 3 d spectrum peaks at binding energies of 182.2 eV (Zr 3 ) and 184.6 eV (Zr 3 ), which is characteristic of Zr 4+ in ZrO 2 [62] , [63] . All films displayed O 1 s spectra with a prominent peak at 530.2 eV indicating oxygen ligated within the metal oxide lattice and suggesting well-oxidized and chemically stable films [64] , [65] , [66] . Additional peaks observed between 531.5–533.5 eV correspond to oxygen vacancies and chemisorbed oxygen associated with carbonates or hydroxyl groups [67] . The presence of oxygen vacancies is especially significant in ₂ ₃ and ₂ ₃ , thus implying a higher defect density that could affect the films’ optical transparency.

Near the connectivity threshold (percolation threshold), small non-monotonic shifts in the Hall-extracted parameters can occur because current paths are not yet fully uniform. For example, in Ga₂O₃/Ag/Ga₂O₃ the 6–8 nm change in the density of free charge carriers (2.75 ×10²¹ to 2.52 ×10²¹ cm⁻³) lies within our experimental uncertainty, and the 10–12 nm mobility decrease is consistent with amorphous and trap-limited transport [71] ; the sheet resistance ( R s ) continues to fall with Ag thickness ( Table 1 ).
FESEM (plan-view) of the O/Ag/O multilayers shows a non-continuous, nanogranular surface (island growth) at lower Ag thicknesses that becomes laterally uniform (continuous) at higher Ag thicknesses ( Fig. 8 ) (Volmer–Weber growth [72] ). In the island regime, charge moves by tunneling between Ag-rich clusters; the inter-cluster gap ( s ) and barrier height ( κ ) set the conductance ( G ) approximately as G ≈ G 0 e −2κs , where G o is a constant (Simmons-type [73] ). Thus, small decreases in s with increasing silver thickness produce exponential increases in G . Once the silver thickness ( d Ag ) exceeds a percolation threshold ( d c ), the clusters coalesce into metallic pathways. In this connected regime, the conductance follows G ∝ ( d Ag −d c ) x (where x is the percolation exponent) as expected for 2D percolation [74] . This system yields a sharp drop in sheet resistance ( R ₛ). Consistent with this, Table 1 shows a clear trend between Ag thickness and the sheet resistance ( R s ), which drops sharply as the Ag thickness increases, and the films evolve from insulating to conductive, matching the FESEM-evidenced shift from discrete islands to continuous morphology .
The degree of Ag clustering varies among different oxide systems, indicating that the interaction between Ag and the oxide matrix significantly influences conductivity. Silver clustering promotes percolation and electron transfer in some oxides. In others, the dispersion of Ag appears to hinder connectivity and reduce charge transfer efficiency. The carrier mobility increased with thicker Ag layers due to improved charge transport and reduced carrier scattering. Mobility in these multilayer structures is affected by competing mechanisms such as scattering at grain boundaries, surface roughness, and possible Ag diffusion within the oxide matrix. Unlike doped oxides, where carrier mobility is typically limited by ionized and neutral impurity scattering, the mobility improvement in Ag-sandwiched films is most likely due to enhanced carrier percolation, formation of metallic conduction pathways, and reduced interfacial charge trapping [75] .
The rise in carrier concentration with increasing Ag thickness is attributed to the availability of free charge carriers through direct transport in the metal layer or Ag diffusion into the oxide layers. Adding Ag to such multilayers also introduces potential interface effects where electron depletion or accumulation at the Ag-oxide interfaces can alter the effective carrier density and transport properties [76] . Therefore, the electrical conductivity mechanism in these multilayer films may involve tunneling through discontinuous Ag layers, direct conduction through percolated Ag networks, and possible Ag diffusion into the oxide layers.
Diffusion among the layers can occur via several mechanisms: the ultrathin nature of the films; the columnar microstructure, which facilitates diffusion; momentum transfer caused by collisions of evaporated species with the growing film, leading to intrusion into the layers; and unintentional substrate heating during e-beam evaporation, which enhances diffusion. The diffusion of Ag into the oxide layers may generate additional charge carriers, but excessive diffusion can produce scattering centers that diminish conductivity gains. The extent of Ag diffusion depends on the oxide matrix and the deposition conditions– these parameters in turn determine whether silver remains as a bounded conductive interlayer or diffuses into the surrounding oxides [77] . The mobility saturates at higher Ag thickness, thus indicating that further Ag addition no longer significantly improves charge transport. This is similar to doped oxides where over-doping causes donor saturation and reduces ionization efficiency [78] .
The type of oxide mainly determines the electrical behavior of the multilayer films, thus emphasizing the role of the oxide-Ag interface in charge transport. ₂ -based films remained insulating even with an 8-nm Ag layer, thus indicating that ZrO ₂ ₂ has a higher percolation threshold. We attribute this to the fact that ZrO₂ has (i) a greater interfacial barrier to electron injection (consistent with its wide E g and low defect density [79] ), (ii) worse Ag wetting [80] that leads to more widely spaced islands for a given nominal thickness, and (iii) reduced Ag interdiffusion during room-temperature deposition, thus collectively shifting the percolation threshold upward to ∼10 nm. However, Ga ₂ O ₃ ₂ ₃ -, Er ₂ O ₃ ₂ ₃ -, HfO ₂ ₂ -, and Ta ₂ O ₅ ₂ ₅ -based films showed a steady decrease in resistivity, thus confirming that these oxides promote better charge transport and Ag connectivity within the multilayer structures. The differences in electrical properties among the oxides imply that interface effects between Ag and the adjacent oxide layers greatly affect charge transport. The Ag layer acts not just as a metallic bridge but also interacts actively with the oxide matrix, thus influencing carrier transport at the nanoscale. These results demonstrate that oxide/Ag/oxide multilayer structures are promising for use in transparent conductive electrodes, plasmonic devices, and tunable electrical materials.

### 3.3. Optical properties

Fig. 9 shows the transmittance spectra of the metal oxide thin films. Ga 2 O 3 had the lowest transparency, which results from its relatively narrower bandgap, as will be explained later. The other metal oxides were transparent across the visible and UV spectral ranges, thus indicating their suitability for UV optoelectronic applications. The presence of maxima and minima– due to the interference of rays reflected at the film-air and film-substrate interfaces– suggests the high optical quality of these films because these interference patterns originate from sharp interfaces.

The absorption coefficient α of a thin film is approximately given by the relation α = 1 / d ln 1 / T , where d is the thickness of the film and T is its transmittance. In the fundamental absorption region, for a direct-bandgap semiconductor, the absorption coefficient is related to the incident photon energy ( E ) by the Tauc relation α E = A E − E g [81] , where A is a constant. Tauc plots ( α E 2 vs. E ) in Fig. 10 , were used to determine the bandgap values by extrapolating the linear regions of the curves to the energy axis. The bandgap values are 4.40 eV, 4.75 eV, 5.56 eV, 5.60 eV, and 5.82 eV for Ga 2 O 3 , Ta 2 O 5 , Er 2 O 3 , ZrO 2 , and HfO 2 thin films, respectively. Our bandgap values align well with the reported values of 4.40–5.11 eV for Ga 2 O 3 [39] , 4.47–4.72 eV for Ta 2 O 5 [36] , 5.60 eV for Er 2 O 3 [40] , 5.80 eV for ZrO 2 [43] , and 5.90 eV for HfO 2 [37] .

To account for the increasing demand for applications operating in the deep UV (DUV) range (λ < 300 nm), we also calculated the FOM values in the broader band ( λ = 200 − 400 nm). The FOM values in this range, as expected, were lower because they include shorter wavelengths where transmittance usually declines. For example, the ZrO ₂ /Ag/ ZrO ₂ (45/12/45 nm) multilayer showed an FOM of 1.17 × 10⁻³ Ω⁻¹ in the [200–400 nm] range compared to 23.76 × 10⁻³ Ω⁻¹ in the 300–400 nm range. Similarly, the HfO ₂ /Ag/ HfO ₂ (45/12/45 nm) system exhibited an FOM of 1.05 × 10⁻³ Ω⁻¹ in the extended range versus 20.77 × 10⁻³ Ω⁻¹ in the narrower UV window. The Er ₂ O ₃ /Ag/ Er ₂ O ₃ (45/12/45 nm) system also followed this trend with its FOM decreasing from 17.60 × 10⁻³ Ω⁻¹ to 1.52 × 10⁻³ Ω⁻¹ . Despite this reduction, the FOM in the extended range still shows good performance and indicates that our structures offer an excellent balance between conductivity and transparency across the entire DUV and UV ranges. This makes them highly promising candidates for use in high-performance UV optoelectronics, lithography, and deep-UV photonics applications.

## 4. Conclusions

This study examined the use of evaporated oxide/silver/oxide (O/Ag/O) multilayers as transparent conductors in the ultraviolet (UV) spectral range and based on the wide-bandgap oxides Er ₂ O ₃ , Ga ₂ O ₃ , HfO ₂ , Ta ₂ O ₅ , and ZrO ₂ . Our results indicate that while adding silver layers improved electrical conductivity, their effect on UV transparency was more complex and varied among the different metal oxides. X-ray diffraction confirmed that the films were amorphous, thus leading to smooth surfaces and sharp interfaces. Surface morphology showed that surface roughness values depended on the type of metal oxide and the thickness of the silver layer. Hall effect measurements revealed that electrical conductivity increased with thicker silver layers, thus aligning with the theory that electron mobility and carrier concentration improved in these structures. Optical tests using UV-Vis spectrophotometry demonstrated that an O/Ag/O configuration can achieve high UV transparency. Among the multilayers studied, the 45/12/45 nm thick ZrO ₂ /Ag/ ZrO ₂ offered the best balance of transparency and conductivity with 85.0 % transmittance at 336 nm, a sheet resistance of 6.0 Ω/◻, an average transmittance of 82.3 % in the wavelength range of 300–400 nm, and an FOM of 23.76 × 10⁻³ Ω⁻¹ . These features make it the most promising candidate for UV optoelectronic applications. This FOM value surpasses previous reports, thus highlighting the competitiveness of our design. We also tested the performance in the extended UV range of 200–400 nm, where all structures had lower FOM values due to absorption at shorter wavelengths. In this extended range, the Er ₂ O ₃ /Ag/ Er ₂ O ₃ (45/8/45 nm) structure showed the best performance with an FOM of 1.69 × 10⁻³ Ω⁻¹ , a sheet resistance of 23.4 Ω/◻, an average transmittance of 72.4 %, and a maximum transmittance of 87.6 % at 330 nm, thus confirming it as the optimal structure for broadband deep UV applications. Accordingly, for practical selection, ZrO₂/Ag/ZrO₂ (45/12/45 nm) is optimal for the 300–400 nm window (FOM 23.76 × 10⁻³ Ω⁻¹) whereas Er₂O₃/Ag/Er₂O₃ (45/8/45 nm) is preferred for the broader 200–400 nm band (FOM 1.69 × 10⁻³ Ω⁻¹).
