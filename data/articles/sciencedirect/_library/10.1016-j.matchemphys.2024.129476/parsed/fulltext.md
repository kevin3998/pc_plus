## Abstract

In light of the three primary challenges faced by transparent conductive coatings, namely poor conductivity, elevated the active substance content, and insufficient visible light transmittance, net-like conductive agents due to the unique morphology and structure have been developed to address these concerns simultaneously. A straightforward synthesis of antimony-doped tin oxide (ATO) nanonets was accomplished through a simple two-step coprecipitation and hydrothermal methods . Firstly, an antimony-doped stannic hydroxide hydrogel precursor was generated through coprecipitation . Secondly, by adjusting the pH value in the hydrothermal reaction, ATO nanocrystals network were obtained without the need for additional materials. The synthesized ATO, with a net-like morphology, comprises particles of approximately 3–5 nm in diameter. Throughout the reaction, ammonium hydroxide , used as a pH regulator, played a vital role as a reducing agent in forming ATO nanonets without the need for further post-treatment. Moreover, net-like ATO compounded with SiO 2 transparent conductive coatings exhibited exceptional transparent conductive properties, with a visible-light transmittance of approximately 84.7 % at the wavelength of 550 nm and a square resistance of 0.5 kΩ/sq upon spin-coating deposition on glass. This remarkable performance can be attributed to the generation of a continuous conductive network. In comparison to granular ATO, the network structure ATO nanocrystal showcases a significant improvement in resistivity, demonstrating its exemplary transparent conductivity. The approach for the preparation of ATO nanonets was simple and easy to scale up to provide an enormous potential and broad prospect for the application of net-like ATO in transparent conductive coating.

## 1. Introduction

Static electricity is an omnipresent and pervasive phenomenon within the realms of industrial production and contemporary lifestyles. Its presence can impede the manufacturing process and potentially lead to dire consequences such as fires and explosions [ 1 ]. Transparent conductive thin films , renowned for their ability to exhibit both optimal optical transparency within the visible electromagnetic spectrum and high electrical conductivity , assume a vital role in the realm of antistatic coatings. As a result of these desirable characteristics, they have garnered significant attention as an exceptional solution for the mitigation of static charges accumulation [ 2 , 3 ]. Moreover, the demand of flexible electronic devices led to the development of coatings with electrical properties. Polymers such as polyurethane (PU), derived from aqueous dispersions, reinforced with conductors have the potential to expand the range of applications, such as sensors, antistatic packaging and conductive coatings [ 4 , 5 ]. Inorganic conductors, acting as conductive agents, generally outperform the organic counterparts due to their superior conductive properties. Noteworthy candidates in this field consist of indium tin oxide (ITO) [ 6 ], fluorine-doped tin oxide (FTO) [ 7 ], metal nanowires [ 8 , 9 ], carbon nanotubes [ 10 ], and graphene films [ 11 , 12 ], all demonstrating great potential in the fabrication of transparent and conductive thin films. Among these contenders, antimony doped tin dioxide (ATO) stands out as an appealing choice due to its contemporary features, including remarkable conductivity, visible light transmittance, cost-effectiveness, antioxidative properties and chemical stability [ [13] , [14] , [15] , [16] , [17] ]. Consequently, its range of applications in the realm of antistatic coatings appears promising.
For the conductive agent, the size and shape are two crucial factors in determining its conductive properties, which are closely linked to the preparation method of the materials. Various approaches have been adopted to prepare ATO nanomaterials , such as spray pyrolysis [ 18 ], sol-gel [ 19 , 20 ], pulsed-laser deposition [ 21 , 22 ], chemical coprecipitation [ 23 ], electron beam evaporation or magnetron sputtering [ 24 ]. However, besides the expensive equipment and complicated preparation process, the morphology of ATO is mostly granular [ [25] , [26] , [27] ], typically exhibiting an isolated structure without connections, which requires a large amount of conductive agent to form a co-continuous conductive pathway in order to achieve an antistatic effect. To some extent, it not only increases the cost but also limits the application range of ATO materials. In contrast, netlike structure materials with a high surface-to-volume ratio or periodicity at the mesoscale have smaller contact resistance and are easier to form a conductive path [ 28 ], which contributes to the enhancement of antistatic properties. Especially in the field of building glass, high transparency and antistatic properties need to be simultaneously met, requiring lower conductive agent content. This poses a challenge for the preparation of net-like morphology ATO nanomaterials. Currently, ATO nanorods or nanowires have been successfully prepared using coprecipitation [ 29 ], hydrothermal [ 30 ], and thermal evaporation methods [ 31 ]. However, the addition of template agents or crystalline inducers in the preparation of one-dimensional materials often leads to complicated and expensive procedures [ [32] , [33] , [34] ], hindering scalability. Therefore, finding a simple and easy large-scale amplification method for the preparation of net-like ATO nanomaterials has become an important area of research.
In this paper, a high stability net-like structure ATO nanomaterial with a diameter of about 3–5 nm was prepared through a two-step approach combining liquid-phase coprecipitation and the hydrothermal method , without the use of any additives or modifiers. The pH value in the hydrothermal reaction was adjusted to achieve the desired morphology. The prepared ATO nanonets have the potential to be used as antistatic coatings or in flexible antistatic polymer composite materials. At the same conductive agent content, the conductive film prepared using ATO nanonets exhibited lower resistance value of 0.5 kΩ/sq and a higher visible-light transmittance of approximately 84.7 % at the wavelength of 550 nm, compared to granular ATO with resistance of 600 kΩ/sq and the transmittance of 85 %. This study is expected to provide a theoretical basis and present a novel tactic for the large-scale preparation of one-dimensional ATO nanonets with excellent conductivity.

### 2.2. Preparation of antimony-doped stannic hydroxide wet gel

The antimony-doped stannic hydroxide wet gel used in this work was prepared using the acetic acid coprecipitation method, as detailed in the literature [ 35 ]. In a volume of 125 mL of acetic acid, introduce 5 g of tin powder, 1 g of antimony trioxide and 0.5 g of citric acid, then stir the mixture at a temperature of 60 °C for a duration 2 h. Subsequently, add 10 mL of hydrogen peroxide to yield a light yellow transparent solution, followed by the addition a certain amount of ammonia to obtain white precipitation. After filtration and washing with deionized water until the conductivity of the filtrate dropped below 50 μS cm −1 , the resulting antimony-doped stannic hydroxide was denoted as ATO hydrogel.

### 2.3. Preparation of nanonet-like ATO crystalline

Antimony-doped stannic hydroxide hydrogel (10.28 g, solid content 17.6 wt %) was added to a beaker containing 50 mL of distilled water and treated with ultrasound in a cell crusher for 30 min under an ice water bath. The pH value of the ATO hydrogel dispersion was measured as 7.0. After undergoing a hydrothermal reaction at 200 °C for 20 h and subsequent washing until the conductivity dropped below 50 μS cm −1 , the ATO nanocrystalline was obtained. To compare different pH values, the ATO hydrogel dispersion was adjusted to pH 8.0, 9.0, 10.0, 11.0, and 12.0 by adding ammonium hydroxide (NH 3 ·H 2 O), peptized for a few hours, followed by the respective hydrothermal reactions. The resulting hydrosol product was denoted as ATO-x, with x representing the different pH values during the hydrothermal reaction. The preparation process for the net-like ATO crystalline is shown in Fig. 1 .

### 2.4. Preparation of ATO/SiO 2 composite coatings

ATO-8 was mixed with SiO 2 hydrosol (SiO 2 : ATO = 14 wt%) to improve the adhesion between the film and substrate. The ATO/SiO 2 composite antistatic coating, denoted as ATO/SiO 2 -8, was prepared by spin coating on a glass substrate. Utilize a pipette to transfer 200 μL of the composite film-forming solution, a mixture of ATO-8 and SiO 2 hydrosol, onto the glass substrate, and then employ a desktop homogenizer to apply the solution via a spin-coating technique. Optimal spinning parameters include a speed of 3000 rpm and a duration of 30 s. Allow the film to cure in an environmental chamber maintained at 40 °C and 40 % relative humidity for a period of 30 min, thereby obtaining a layer of ATO-based antistatic film. This process can be iterated to achieve films of differing thicknesses. Subsequently, the dried ATO film is transferred to a muffle furnace and subjected to a controlled heating sequence in static air, incrementing the temperature by 1 °C per minute to reach 550 °C, and maintained at this temperature for 30 min. Then, the film is allowed to cool naturally to ambient temperature, completing the fabrication of the ATO/SiO 2 -8 antistatic film. For comparison, ATO-7 without adjusting the dispersion acidity in the hydrothermal reaction, mixed with SiO 2 , was also prepared using the same coating method and denoted as ATO/SiO 2 -7.

### 2.5. Structure characterization

The morphology and crystal structure of prepared ATO nanocrystalline was investigated by a transmission electron microscope operated at 200 kV, electron beam generated from a LaB 6 gun (TEM, JEOL JEM-2100). X-ray diffraction (XRD) data was collected on a Bruker D8 Advance X-ray diffractometer operated at 40 kV and 40 mA with Cu-Kα radiation at a wavelength of 0.15406 nm and a scan range of 15–80° with a step size of 0.02° and a dwell time of 0.3 s. An FZ-2010 powder resistivity tester (Shanghai Yiyu Instrument Co., Ltd) was used to analyze the resistivity of ATO freeze-dried powder. Volume resistivity, characterizing the electrical properties of a material, is calculated from the Ohm's law. For the mold utilized in our tests, the formula for determining volume resistivity is ρ = R· πD 2 /4 L, where ρ represents the volume resistivity (Ω·cm), R denotes the resistance value (Ω), D is the diameter of the conductive powder sample pool (cm), and L indicates the column height of the conductive powder sample (cm). Zeta potential of the ATO-x hydrosol was analyzed using a zeta potential analyzer (Nano ZS, Malvern, UK). Stability of the ATO-x hydrosol was analyzed using a stability analyzer (LUMI-Sizer, Germany) with a test temperature of 25 °C, test time of 24 h, and a speed of 4000 rpm. Potentiometric titration was performed using a charge titration system (Stabino, Particle Metrix, Germany), by using sodium hydroxide or hydrochloric acid as titrants, the pH value of the sample system was determined alongside the zeta potential variation. The ultraviolet visible light (UV–vis) spectra of ATO hydrogel with different acidity in water at the wavelength of 550 nm was measured with an UV-2600 spectrophotometer (Shimadzu Corporation, Japan). The square resistance of the conductive ATO/SiO 2 composition coatings was measured with an RTS-9 dual-electric measurement four-probe tester (Guangzhou 4Probes Tech., Ltd). The cross sections of the ATO/SiO 2 coating films was analyzed with a scanning electron microscope (Nova Nano SEM 450, FEI Company, U.S.A.). Upon sectioning the glass sheet coated with ATO/SiO 2 films using a glass knife, the thickness of the layered film on the newly prepared cross-section is examined. The metallographic morphology of ATO/SiO 2 coatings was investigated by three-dimensional optical profilometer (Contour GTK, Bruker, U.S.A.). NMR solvent relaxation was measured on a particle interface characteristic analyzer, operating at 13 MHz (XiGo Nanotools, Inc., Bethlehem, PA, U.S.A.). The electromechanical performance of the ATO/PU-8 and ATO/PU-7 composites membrane with dimensions of 20 mm × 5 mm × 1.8 mm was measured with a SANS CMT4000 universal testing machine at a constant rate of 3 mm/min and a Keithley 6487 picoammeter simultaneously at a voltage of 7 V, test current 25 mA, silver paint, stretching 50 % of the length of the spline itself.

## 3. Results and discussion


### 3.1. ATO-x nanocrystalline morphology

In this study, a simple two-step route was developed to fabricate ATO nanonets. The first step involved preparing the precursor antimony-doped stannic hydroxide wet gel using the acetic acid precipitation method, resulting in the ATO hydrogel. The second step involved adjusting the pH value for the hydrothermal reaction to obtain net-like ATO nanocrystals . Ammonium hydroxide (NH 3 ·H 2 O) was used as an acidity regulator and a reducing agent to form ATO nanonets without any further post-treatment. As shown in Fig. 2 , the morphology of ATO-7, without adjusting the pH value, was primarily granular and prone to agglomeration ( Fig. 2 a). When the pH of the ATO hydrogel dispersion was adjusted to 8.0, the agglomeration of nanoparticles was reduced, and the dispersion degree significantly improved. At the same time, ATO nanonets were formed by connecting the particles ( Fig. 2 b). Further increasing the dosage of NH 3 ·H 2 O and adjusting the ATO hydrogel dispersion liquid to pH values of 9.0, 10.0, 11.0, and 12.0, respectively, did not significantly change the morphology of the ATO-x nanocrystals, and the interconnected net-like structure of ATO was maintained ( Fig. 2 c–f). The transmission electron microscope panorama of ATO-8 revealed that the nanocrystals of ATO possessed an average diameter of approximately 3–5 nm, with a distribution of net-like structure ( Fig. 2 g). Additionally, hydrothermal reactions with pH values of 4 and 6 regulated by acetic acid resulted in a granular morphology for ATO, as shown in Fig. S1 . To directly observe the aggregation of ATO with different morphologies, optical photographs of the freeze-dried ATO-x powder obtained from hydrothermal reactions at different pH values, with the same weight of 0.0500 ± 0.0005 g, were shown in Fig. 2 g. It can be observed that the sample height of ATO-8 was the highest, indicating a lower packing density of the net-like structure compared to the granular structure of ATO-7, which tended to accumulate densely due to its optimal distribution of ATO [ 36 ].

In other words, the formation mechanism of ATO nanocrystals belongs to the self-assembly of zero-dimensional structures.

### 3.2. Stability and structural characteristics of ATO-x hydrosols

The stability of ATO-x hydrosols prepared through hydrothermal reactions at different pH values was investigated, as depicted in Fig. 3 a. The y-axis represents the instability index, with a larger value indicating greater instability in the solution. As shown in Fig. 3 a, after 24 h of centrifugation at 4000 rpm, the instability index of ATO-7 hydrosol reached its maximum value of 0.770. With an increase in pH value during the hydrothermal reaction, the instability index of ATO-x initially decreased and then gradually increased. Additionally, the instability index of net-like ATO nanocrystals was generally smaller than that of granular ATO-7 nanocrystals, suggesting that the former possessed greater stability. The ATO-8 nanocrystals exhibited the lowest instability index of 0.243. The Zeta potential of ATO-x was also evaluated, as illustrated in Fig. 3 b. A higher absolute Zeta potential value signified enhanced stability of the hydrosol. Among the various ATO samples, ATO-8 exhibited the maximum absolute Zeta potential value of 53 mV, thereby supporting the deduction that the ATO-8 was the greatest stability of the sample, aligning harmoniously with the stability analysis depicted in Fig. 3 a. Furthermore, the fluctuation in the potential of ATO-7 due to varying pH values was determined through potentiometric titration ( Fig. S2 ). Within the neutral ATO-7 hydrosol environment, the pH was initially adjusted to 10.5 by introducing sodium hydroxide, and then gradually reduced to a pH value of 3.0 by the addition of hydrochloric acid , while simultaneously monitoring the potential alteration. The results revealed that the potential reached its utmost negativity at a pH of about 8, signifying superior stability of the sample. Moreover, the UV–visible spectrophotometer was employed to assess the absorbance of ATO-x nanocrystals in the hydrothermal solution as the pH level increased. The visible absorption spectra of ATO-x nanocrystals are presented in Fig. S3 . As the pH rose during the hydrothermal reaction, the dispersions of ATO-x experienced a lighter color, indicating a decline in carrier concentration and a deterioration in the conductivity of the ATO samples [ 39 ].

Lighter blue color indicated lower Sb doping and worse electrical conductivity . The particle interface characteristics between ATO hydrogel particles and the liquid solvent at different peptization time were measured using a particle interface characteristic analyzer [ 40 ]. NMR solvent relaxation measurement is sensitive to the degree of aggregation in a dispersion. Through the nuclear magnetic resonance method , the relaxation time of hydrogen protons adsorbed on the surface of ATO hydrogel particles was recorded, which characterized the affinity and surface chemical properties between solid particles and the liquid solvent in a suspended state. In a relaxation experiment, liquid molecules in a dispersion undergo rapid exchange between bound states at the interface and highly mobile free states in the bulk liquid. As long as the surface and bulk liquids are in fast exchange, the observed relaxation time, t av , is determined from the reciprocal of the spin relaxation rate, R av [ 41 ]. The relationship between relaxation time and specific relaxation rate (R sp ) was described by the following equation [ 40 , 41 ]: (1) R s p = ( R a v / R b ) − 1 (2) R s p = ( t b / t a v ) − 1 Where R av is the average spin relaxation rate, R b is the relaxation rate for the free or bulk liquid. t b and t av represented the average spin relaxation time of the solvent and the sample, respectively. For a given dispersion, R sp was directly proportional to the thickness of the bound layer. A larger value of R sp indicated a stronger interaction between the particle and solvent medium, resulting in faster gel dissolution.
At different pH values, the change in R sp and transmittance of ATO hydrogel with different peptization times was shown in Fig. 4 . Generally, the peptization reaction of particles was a surface reaction. In an alkaline solution, the particles initially diminished in size, at the same time, the binding force and affinity between the solvent and particles were enhanced, resulting in an increase in R sp . At the beginning of the peptization reaction, R sp increased with the increase in pH value, indicating the reaction between ATO hydrogel and alkali. The corresponding R sp values for ATO hydrogel samples at pH values of 7, 8, 9, and 10 were 4.5, 6.8, 8.2, and 9.1, respectively. However, the R sp of ATO hydrogel samples at the pH value of 11 decreased to 6.8. This decline could be attributed to the fact that, after 2 min of testing, some particles in the ATO hydrogel at the pH value of 11 had already reacted with the alkali, resulting in a decrease in the number of particles in the solution and a lower R sp value. Subsequently, the variation curve of R sp with peptization time showed a rapid decline followed by stabilization. Similarly, when the peptization reaction was carried out at the pH of 10, the R sp curve showed a downward trend as the particle dissolution accelerated. The curve eventually stabilized after 12 h of peptization reaction time ( Fig. 4 a).
As the pH value increased during the peptization reaction, the transmittance of ATO hydrogel increased ( Fig. 4 b). This indicates that a higher alkali concentration leads to a faster peptization reaction rate and enhanced interaction between the alkaline solution and antimony-doped stannic hydroxide. Consequently, the higher the pH value, the more rapidly the ATO hydrogel dissolved, and the easier it to form a transparent solution. The contemporary fundamental principles governing crystallization are categorized into two distinct modes: the classical Ostwald ripening crystal growth mode, characterized by atom-by-atom additions to an existing nucleus or dissolution of unstable phases followed by reprecipitation of more stable phases, and the oriented attachment mechanism, which involves particle-based aggregation modes where adjacent nanoparticles self-assemble by sharing a common crystallographic orientation and aligning at a planar interface. The driving force behind this spontaneous oriented attachment is the reduction in surface free energy resulting from the elimination of pairs of high-energy surfaces from a thermodynamic perspective [ 42 ].
The zeta potential of antimony-doped stannic hydroxide hydrogel at various pH values is depicted in Fig. S4 . The point of zero net proton charge for antimony-doped stannic hydroxide is approximately 3. When the pH exceeds 3, the corresponding zeta potential becomes negative, indicating a significant negative charge on the colloidal particle surfaces. According to colloidal theory, when the absolute value of the zeta potential is around 30 mV [ 43 ], the repulsion between particles is stronger, leading to better dispersion. At a pH above 4.5, the zeta potential drops below −30 mV, suggesting a uniform dispersion of the sol particles of antimony-doped stannic hydroxide. By passing a laser beam through a dispersion of antimony-doped stannic hydroxide gel with a pH above 7, a clear Tyndall phenomenon is observed, signifying that alkali addition can cause peptizing of the antimony-doped stannic hydroxide (inset of Fig. S4 ).
Modifications in the pH value of the peptization reaction can influence both the dispersion state of antimony-doped tin hydroxide wet gel and the surface charge of colloidal particles. The TEM image of Fig. 1 h in the manuscript can be seen ATO nanonets form through the oriented attachment mechanism. When two nanoparticles closely approach each other, they are mutually attracted by van der Waals forces but can reorganize to achieve a low-energy configuration represented by a coherent particle-particle interface [ 42 ]. ATO nanonets are formed as adjacent nanoparticles self-assemble by sharing a common (110) crystallographic orientation of SnO 2 and docking at a planar interface. There are reports in the literature that many of the materials that have been observed to grow by oriented attachment mechanism are relatively insoluble [ [44] , [45] , [46] ], and an elevated pH in the solution or prolonged peptizing time enhance the solubility of colloidal particles [ 47 ]. As the rate of Ostwald ripening is linked to aqueous ion concentration, the importance of oriented attachment mechanism increases when dissolution is restricted. Both mechanisms can coexist, with the dominant pathway affected by solution chemistry, particle surface conditions, and temperature [ 48 ]. The balance between growth pathways influences coarsening rates and product morphology [ 47 ]. In other words, oriented attachment mechanism of growth becomes more discernible in systems containing relatively insoluble materials, with the oriented attachment growth of sol particles being intricately linked to the pH level of the solution.

### 3.4. ATO/SiO 2 composite antistatic coatings

The metallographic morphology and cross-section images of ATO/SiO 2 -8 coatings with different numbers of spin coating were displayed in Fig. 7 . With 3, 5, 10, 15, 20, and 25 layers of spin coating , the corresponding film thicknesses were 78.3 nm, 120.6 nm, 146.4 nm, 183.8 nm, 256.6 nm, and 366.2 nm, respectively. As the number of spin coatings increased, the thickness of ATO/SiO 2 -8 composite films increased and the coating surface was relatively flat. In comparison to the granular-like ATO/SiO 2 -7 coatings, the conductivity and transmittance of the composite films with different layer numbers were tested ( Fig. 8 ). As the number of spin coating layers increased, the square resistance of the films initially decreased then increased, while the transmittance of the films decreased. For ATO/SiO 2 -8, the coatings had 5 layers, corresponding to a film thickness of 120.6 nm. This resulted in the lowest square resistance of 0.5 kΩ/sq and a transmittance of 84.7 % at the wavelength of 550 nm in the visible region, indicating the obtained film exhibited relatively good uniformity and optimal conductivity ( Fig. 8 a). When the film exceeded 10 layers, the resistance value increased, and the transmittance decreased, suggesting that the film thickness had become thickening, leading to unevenness and potential cracking, which was consistent with the analysis in Fig. 7 c–f. Similar to the net-like ATO/SiO 2 -8 film, the resistance of the granular-like ATO/SiO 2 -7 decreased initially and then increased with the increase in spin coating layers. However, the resistivity value was approximately two orders of magnitude higher than that of the net-like structured ATO/SiO 2 -8. The ATO/SiO 2 -7 coating with 10 layers possessed a smaller resistivity of 150 kΩ/sq and a transmittance of 76 % at the wavelength of 550 nm ( Fig. 8 b).

Moreover, the net-like structured ATO-8 was combined with waterborne polyurethane (PU), and the potential application in antistatic flexible sensors was preliminarily discussed (see supporting information for preparation methods). ATO/PU composites resin alterations in resistance value during the stretching process were monitored through a universal testing machine. The ATO polymer composite material was stretched to 50 % of its length at a consistent rate of 3 mm/min, subsequent to which the force was released to retract it to its initial length, thereby assessing the resistance variability of the composite material under dynamic conditions. The ATO-8 and polyurethane composition (with an additive amount of 5 wt%) displayed a lower resistance of 255 kΩ/sq in contrast to the ATO-7 and polyurethane composition, which recorded a resistance of 400 kΩ/sq ( Fig. S5a ). At the same amount of 5 wt% ATO, the initial resistance of the net-like ATO/PU-8 composite was measured at 3 × 10 6 Ω, with a duration of approximately 50 s for a single tension-rebound cycle. In contrast, ATO/PU-7 composite exhibited an initial resistance of 6.6 × 10 6 Ω, with a cycle duration of around 80 s ( Fig. S5b ). The experimental results reveal that the ATO/PU-8 is responsive to resistance changes under strain conditions, and it is anticipated for application in fields necessitating deformation sensitivity, such as sensors and electronic skin. This was attributed to the net-like structure of ATO-8, which uniformly distributed within the polyurethane matrix and formed the desired conductive network ( Fig. S6 ).

## 4. Summary

The morphology and structure of ATO nanocrystalline can be effectively controlled by adjusting the pH value of the hydrothermal reaction using antimony-doped stannic hydroxide as a precursor, which affected the grain size and the crystallinity of the ATO production. The net-like structured ATO nanocrystalline with a diameter of approximately 3–5 nm was prepared in an alkaline solution adjusted by NH 3 ·H 2 O. The net-like structure ATO-8 showed excellent stability and low resistivity. Meanwhile, the ATO nanocrystalline was combined with SiO 2 hydrosol and polyurethane to construct antistatic coatings and flexible polymers, respectively. Compared with the antistatic nanomaterials prepared by granular-like ATO-7, the net-like ATO-8 exhibited advantages such as less addition, better electrical conductivity, higher visible light transmittance. This demonstrates a promising prospect for commercial application, attributed to the superior properties of the net-like structured ATO-8.
